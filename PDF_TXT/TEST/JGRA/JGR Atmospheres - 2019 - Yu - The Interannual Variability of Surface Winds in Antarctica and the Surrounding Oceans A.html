(ERA‐Interim) reanalysis for 1979–2017 and the empirical orthogonal function (EOF) technique. The ﬁrst
three EOF modes, which account for 18–23%, 11–15%, and 8–10% of the total variances for the four
austral seasons, are related to several known modes of Southern Hemisphere large‐scale circulation
including the southern annular mode, Paciﬁc South American (PSA), and Zonal Wave 3 (ZW3) and to

Speciﬁcally, EOF1 and EOF2 are signiﬁcantly correlated with southern annular mode in all seasons
except for autumn EOF2. Neither EOF1 nor EOF2 are signiﬁcantly correlated to Niño 3, but Niño 4 is

EOF1 and PSA2. EOF3 is signiﬁcantly correlated with Niño 3, Niño 4, and PSA2 in all seasons but
summer. The spatial patterns of all three modes can be largely explained by the anomalous mean sea
level pressure ﬁelds that strengthen or weaken climatological winds in different regions of the Antarctica
and the Southern Ocean, although variations in katabatic forcing also plays a role in the variations
of surface winds especially in East Antarctica and during winter season where and when katabatic
forcing dominates.
1. Introduction
Surface winds in Antarctica, which are generally strong and persistent in direction especially in winter,
are driven by a combination of large‐scale pressure gradient force, katabatic force on long, ice‐covered
slopes and other local topographic effects (Parish & Cassano, 2003). These strong surface winds can
inﬂuence the spatial and temporal variations of snow accumulation and modulate Antarctic mass balance
(King et al., 2004). The variations of these surface winds have been linked to the variations of the
tropospheric polar vortex and westerly winds over the Southern Ocean (Yasunari & Kodama, 1993).
The latter can affect upwelling of deep waters in the Southern Ocean and thereby the melt of ice shelf in
West Antarctica (Turner et al., 2017). The changes in the Antarctic surface winds have been found to exert
a control through dynamic and thermodynamic processes on the trend in Antarctic sea ice concentrations
(Holland & Kwok, 2012). Hence, it is crucial to understand the multiscale variability of Antarctic
surface winds.
Numerous studies have examined the characteristics of Antarctic surface winds and the relative role of
large‐scale pressure gradient forcing, katabatic forcing on ice covered slopes, and terrain blocking and
channeling in shaping the winds and their spatial and seasonal distribution (Parish & Cassano, 2003; van
den Broeke & van Lipzig, 2003). The climatological Antarctic surface wind ﬁeld has been simulated success-
fully by mesoscale numerical models, though simulated surface winds are underestimated over steep slopes
(Bintanja et al., 2014a; Powers et al., 2003; Sanz Rodrigo et al., 2013). Most observational studies focused on
speciﬁc sites or a small region in Antarctica (Knuth & Cassano, 2011; Nigro & Cassano, 2014; Savijärvi, 2011;
Speirs et al., 2010). While most of these site‐speciﬁc studies examined temporal variability of surface winds

Some of the current understanding on the Antarctic surface wind inter-
annual variability came from studies of the effects of ENSO on Antarctic
climate. Bromwich et al. (2004) employed polar Mesoscale Model
(MM5) to investigate the impact of ENSO on Antarctic climate in the late
1990s and found opposite variability of surface meridional winds between
the Ross Ice Shelf‐Marie Byrd Land and the Weddell Sea‐Ronne/Filchner
Ice Shelf. Surface warm northerly (cold southerly) winds over the
Antarctic Peninsula (Ross/Amundsen Seas) promote the dipole structure
of Antarctic sea ice concentration anomalies associated with the
cold phase of ENSO (Holland et al., 2005; Simpkins et al., 2012;
Stammerjohn et al., 2008). The observed increasing trend in Antarctic
summertime sea ice cover is also related to the anomalous surface wind
ﬁelds associated with La Niña (Yu et al., 2018). Although ENSO is a major
climate variability mode on interannual scale, other climate modes affect-
ing high southern latitude climate, such as SAM, may also inﬂuence surface winds in different regions of
Antarctica and the surrounding oceans. The variability of surface westerly winds over the Southern Ocean
has been linked to the polarity of SAM (Hartmann & Lo, 1998; Thompson & Wallace, 2000), with stronger
westerly jet corresponding to positive phase SAM and vice versa (Thompson et al., 2011). Swart et al. (2015),
however, suggested that the trends in SAM are not a direct proxy for changes in the strength of the westerly
jet. More studies are needed to fully understand the inﬂuence of SAM on the surface wind over Antarctica.
Warming due to greenhouse gas emissions also is an important factor for the changes in Antarctic
surface winds. High‐resolution climate simulations with the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change
emissions Scenario A (Cubasch et al., 1992) resulted in a 10% increase in katabatic wind speeds over
Antarctica in winter and a 15% decrease in summer between the present and the midcentury (2040–2050)
climate (van den Broeke et al., 1997). Surface wind speeds over Antarctica showed a small decrease in the
future period (2094–2098) compared to those in present time (2002–2006; Bintanja et al., 2014b), attributable
mainly to the changes in the synoptic forcing, not in the katabatic forcing.
In this study, we investigate systematically the interannual variability of surface (10 m) wind speeds over
Antarctica. Our analyses extend the previous site‐based studies of surface wind variability spatially from a
few locations or a small region to the entire Antarctic continent and surrounding oceans and temporally
from a few years to nearly four decades. Besides contributing to the understanding of the regional differences
of the interannual variability, our analyses also reveal how the interannual variability changes with season.
The rational for examining the season dependency of surface wind interannual variability is that large‐scale
climate variability modes, such as SAM and ENSO, have been shown to affect Antarctic climate differently
in different seasons (Simpkins et al., 2012) and that previous studies on surface wind variability in Antarctica
(Holland et al., 2005; Stammerjohn et al., 2008; Yu et al., 2018) have focused mostly on a speciﬁc season.
The overall purpose of this study is to identify the modes of interannual variability of surface wind speeds
over Antarctica and the surrounding oceans for each season of the year and to determine if there is a
statistically signiﬁcant connection of these modes to the known modes of large‐scale circulation that
inﬂuence the Southern Hemisphere climate.
The rest of the manuscript is organized as follows: Data used for the analyses and the statistical methods
applied to the data are described in the next section. Section 3 presents the results, beginning with spatial pat-
terns of seasonal mean wind speeds for each season, followed by interannual variability as revealed by the
leading empirical orthogonal function (EOF) modes and the relationship of these modes to known large‐scale
circulation modes. The results are summarized in section 4 along with a more extended discussion.

2. Data Sets and Methods
The domain of this analysis is the region south of latitude 60°S including the Antarctic Continent and the
Southern Ocean (Figure 1). Surface (10 m) wind speeds over the domain are obtained from the European
Centre for Medium‐Range Weather Forecasts Interim (ERA‐Interim) reanalysis (Dee et al., 2011). The
gridded surface wind data from ERA‐Interim reanalysis have a temporal resolution of four times per day
on a reduced Gaussian grid with a horizontal resolution of approximately 80 km. For the current analysis,
the data from the original grid are interpolated onto a latitude and longitude grid of 0.4° by 0.4°. As an
updated version of an earlier reanalysis (ERA‐40, Uppala et al., 2005), ERA‐Interim was produced with more
available observational data, especially satellite data (Dee et al., 2011), better bias correction algorithms
(Haimberger et al., 2008; Vasiljevic, 2006; Vasiljevic et al., 2006), and greatly improved data assimilation
method. The result is a data set of better quality including, relevant to the current study, an overall decrease
of 14% in normalized mean absolute error of the Antarctic surface wind speed relative to ERA‐40. However,
ERA‐Interim still contains a large wind speed bias in escarpment and coastal area (Rodrigo et al., 2013) that
was attributed largely to the underrepresentation of katabatic winds rather than geostrophic winds of synop-
tic circulations (Klöwer et al., 2013). The ERA‐Interim surface wind ﬁelds were also found to be better than
those of the Hadley Global Environment Model 2 (Collins et al., 2011), due to a higher horizontal resolution
of ERA‐Interim (Klöwer et al., 2013).

The four‐times daily wind speed data are averaged to obtain daily wind speed means, which are further
averaged to yield seasonal wind speed means for austral autumn (March–May [MAM]), austral winter
(June–August [JJA]), austral spring (September–November [SON]), and austral summer (December–
February [DJF]). Notice here that daily and seasonal means of wind speeds are scalar averaged instead of
vector averaged. The interannual variability of seasonal mean winds at each grind point is obtained by
subtracting the seasonal means averaged over the 39‐year (1979–2017) study period from the seasonal means
of each year. The EOF method (Wilks, 2011) is applied to the seasonal wind speed anomalies in the study
domain to identify the main modes of variability.
To put the interannual variability of Antarctic surface winds in the context of large‐scale circulation, several
climate indices are calculated using the 500‐hPa geopotential height data from ERA‐Interim. The SAM index
and the Paciﬁc South American (PSA) Modes 1 and 2 are deﬁned respectively as the ﬁrst three EOF modes of
seasonal 500‐hPa geopotential height anomalies (Mo & Higgins, 1998). The Zonal Wave Number 3 (ZW3)
index describes the spatial and temporal variability of the 500‐hPa geopotential height anomalies in the
southern high latitude with Wave Number 3 (Raphael, 2004). In addition to the above indices related to
500‐hPa geopotential height anomalies in southern high latitude regions, two other indices, Niño 3 and
Niño 4, representing respectively the Eastern and Central Paciﬁc sea surface temperature (SST) anomalies
on interannual scale, are downloaded from the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration
climate data center (http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/data/indices/) and used in the current analyses.
Finally, the current analyses also utilize, besides the ERA‐Interim data set, the gridded (2° by 2° longitude
and latitude grid) SST data (Huang et al., 2017; http://ftp.cdc.noaa.gov/noaa.ersst.v5). The potential
connection between the large‐scale climate variability modes and the leading EOF modes of surface wind

3.2. Interannual Variability of Seasonal Mean Winds
EOF analysis is used to identify the leading modes of surface wind speed variability for each season. Table 1
exhibits the percentages of variances explained by the ﬁrst four EOF modes. According to the criterion of
North et al. (1982), the ﬁrst three modes are signiﬁcantly different from each other and from the other
modes. Thus, we only analyze the spatial patterns (EOFs) and time series (PCs) of the ﬁrst three modes
and their related large‐scale circulation states.
3.2.1. The First Mode
The variance explained by the ﬁrst EOF mode (EOF1) varies between 18% for austral autumn and 23% for
austral spring (Table 1). The EOF1 spatial patterns are similar for austral spring and summer with positive
wind speed anomalies dominating most of the domain (Figure 5) except for coastal region of East Antarctica,
the Amundsen Sea and some inland areas of West Antarctica where the values are negative. The EOF1
patterns for austral autumn and winter are similar but opposite to those in spring and summer with mostly
negative anomalies over the Antarctic continent, the Ross and Amundsen Seas, and parts of the southern
Indian Ocean and positive elsewhere in the domain.

4. Conclusion and Discussion
In this study, the interannual variability and its seasonal dependency of the mean surface wind speeds in
Antarctica and the Southern Ocean is investigated using the gridded ERA‐Interim reanalysis data for
1979–2017 and EOF analyses.
The ﬁrst three EOF modes reveal 18–23%, 11–15%, and 8–10% of the total variances in the surface wind
speeds for the four austral seasons. Both the ﬁrst (EOF1) and the second (EOF2) modes have a statistically
signiﬁcant correlation with the SAM index, with the exception of the austral autumn EOF2. Neither EOF1
nor EOF2 are signiﬁcantly correlated to Niño 3, but Niño 4 is signiﬁcantly correlated to EOF1 in austral win-
ter and to EOF2 in all seasons but winter. This difference in the relationships of the two modes with Niño 4 is
largely consistent with the ﬁnding that both modes are strongly connected to SAM and that there is a close
association between SAM and the SST anomalies over the central Paciﬁc for austral winter for EOF1 and for
austral spring and summer for EOF2.
Differences also exist in the regression pattern of the 500‐hPa height anomalies between the two modes, with
signiﬁcantly large anomaly values over the southeastern Paciﬁc Ocean with EOF1 but over East Antarctica
for EOF2. No connection is found between EOF2 and the ZW3 index, but signiﬁcant correlations exist
between EOF1 and ZW3 for all seasons except for summer. A strong correlation is found between EOF2
and PSA1 for spring and winter and between EOF1 and PSA2 for spring.
Although both modes are signiﬁcantly connected to SAM, the impact of SAM on the surface winds, however,
is somewhat different between the two modes. For EOF2, positive SAM corresponds to positive (negative)
surface wind speed anomalies over oceans (land) for summer and winter. A similar pattern occur for
EOF1 in autumn and winter but for spring and summer the wind speed anomalies for EOF1 are mostly posi-
tive during the positive phase of SAM.
The third mode (EOF3) is signiﬁcantly correlated with the Niño 3, Niño 4, and PSA2 indices in all seasons
but summer. For all three modes, the spatial pattern can be largely explained by the anomalous pressure
ﬁelds that either enhance or oppose the climatological pressure gradient forces depending on the region.
A word of caution is necessary here. The aforementioned relationships between large‐scale climate indices
and the surface wind speed anomalies in Antarctica and the surrounding oceans are purely statistical and do
not imply cause and effect.
Recent studies have noted the zonally asymmetrical structure of the SAM index (Fan, 2007; Fogt et al., 2012;
Jones et al., 2009; Neff et al., 2008). The results here also show the zonal asymmetry that divides the spatial
structure of the SAM into two parts: West Antarctica and its surrounding oceans and East Antarctica. Each
part of the SAM spatial pattern corresponds to different atmospheric circulation, surface wind speed pattern
and the relationship with SST anomalies over the tropical Paciﬁc Ocean. Yu et al. (2015) found that the cen-
tral Paciﬁc El Niño during austral spring can excite both the SAM and PSA1 through tropospheric and strato-
spheric processes. This is supported by the correlations in Table 3 that suggest an existence of a signiﬁcant
relationship among Niño 4, SAM, and PSA1. Consequently, our results help advance the understanding of
the structure of the SAM and its relationship with the SST in the tropical Paciﬁc Ocean.
Although surface winds in the ERA‐Interim reanalysis have shown improvement over earlier ERA reanaly-
sis (Rodrigo et al., 2013) and other similar reanalysis products (Klöwer et al., 2013), the relatively low hori-
zontal resolution underestimates the effect of sloping terrain on local wind speed, especially over the coastal
regions of East Antarctica. Recently, Jones and Lister (2015) noted that ERA‐Interim shows a warm bias (up
to 5 °C) at some inland station and a cool bias (up to 6 °C) at some coastal stations. These biases that are
opposite in sign between inland and coastal regions may have large effects on local and regional pressure
gradients that in turn, affect surface winds.
In spite of these limitations, the results here provide an understanding of the main modes of the interannual
variability of the surface wind speeds in Antarctica and the Southern Ocean for each of the four seasons and
the potential connection to the known modes of large‐scale climate variability. This understanding could
facilitate the exploitation of Antarctic wind energy. The results about the spatial pattern of the SAM and
its relation with the SST in the tropical Paciﬁc Ocean improve the knowledge of the SAM structure and
the interactions between tropics and southern middle‐high latitudes.
