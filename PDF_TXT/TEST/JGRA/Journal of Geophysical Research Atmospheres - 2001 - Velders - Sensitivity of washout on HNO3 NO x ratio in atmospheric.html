Sensitivity of washout on HNO3/NO ratio in atmospheric
chemistry transport models
Guus J. M. Velders (cid:127)
Air Research Laboratory, National Institute of Public Health and the Environment (RIVM), Bilthoven, Netherlands
Claire Granier 2'3
Aeronomy Laboratory, NOAA, Boulder, Colorado
Abstract. Global chemistry transport models often overestimate the HNO,(cid:127)/NOx ratio in the
free troposphere compared with observations. The sensitivity of the HNO3/NOx ratio to the
parameterization of washout is studied using two global models, IMAGES and MOZART.
The observed ratio of HNO3/NOx is about 4+3 in the free troposphere over remote areas.
Calculations with the IMAGES model are in good agreement with these observations, but
those with the MOZART model are too high (about 7-20). The good agreement found for
IMAGES might be fortuitous, caused by the physically unrealistic parameterization of
washout as a continuous removal rate. Applying different magnitudes of a continuous
washout or different intermittent washout frequencies
increases the HNO3/NOx ratio by about
a factor of 2-3, worsening the agreement with observations. Smaller, but significant, changes
of the order of 10-20% can be seen in NO, NO2, and ozone as a result of changes in the
washout parameterization. The heterogeneous conversion of HNO3 on SO4 (used as a proxy
for soot) yields only a small improvement in the HNO3/NOx ratio for most remote locations.
Significant effects of this heterogeneous conversion can be expected over and downwind of
polluted locations. Given the sensitivity of the HNO3/NOx ratio to the parameterization of
washout, this ratio may be more suited to test the quality of the parameterization of the
washout than the nitrogen chemistry in global chemistry transport models. Expressed
differently, the HNO3/NOx ratio is little affected by and not very important for tropospheric
chemistry in these models.
1.
Introduction
Nitrogen oxides (NOx = NO + NO2) play a central role in
the production and destruction of ozone in the troposphere.
Their mixing ratios vary by several orders of magnitude both
spatially and temporally. In polluted areas in the boundary
layer the mixing ratio is largely determined by emissions,
transport, and dynamics of the boundary layer height and can
reach values of tens of ppbv. In the free troposphere, away
from source regions, the NOx mixing ratio depends mainly on
the local chemistry and is of the order of 10-50 parts per
trillion by volume (pptv). The HNO3/NO(cid:127) ratio maybe used to
test the NO(cid:127) chemistry in the free troposphere without consid-
ering the absolute values of the nitrogen species, as the
HNO3/NO(cid:127) ratio is relatively little affected by the total NOr
source strength. Observations of the HNO.(cid:127)/NO(cid:127)
ratio in the

2000; Liu et al., 1992; Chatfield, 1994; Fan et al., 1994;

In this paper we study the sensitivity of the HNO3/NOx
ratio in two global three-dimensional chemistry transport
models with respect to the parameterization of the washout.
The models used are an improved version of IMAGES (ver-
sion 4.0) [Granier et al., 2000b] with detailed chemistry and
monthly averaged prescribed dynamics and a new version
(2.0) of the MOZART model [Brasseur et al., 1998;
Hauglustaine et al., 1998] with the same detailed chemistry
but with 6 hourly varying dynamics from a global circulation
emissions database
model. Both models use the EDGAR
[Olivier et al., 1996] for anthropogenic emissions and the
database developed by Miiller [1992] for natural emissions.
The IMAGES model has been used for several sensitivity
studies since it requires far less computer time than the
MOZART model. Washout is parameterized in IMAGES by a
removal rate which varies locally and monthly but is applied
continuously in the model. We have varied the magnitude of
the continuous removal and have also applied several discon-
tinuous washout parameterizations. We consider the effects
on the HNO3/NO(cid:127) ratio on HNO3, NO, NO2, and ozone. The
effects on the possible conversion of HNO3 to NO on soot
have been explored as well. In section 2 a short description of

Hauglustaine et al. [1996] showed that the heterogeneous
conversion of HNO3 to NO on black carbon improved the
agreement between the measured and modeled HNO3/NOx
ratio in MOZART (version 1). They also addressed the effect
of washout of HNO3 on the ratio and found that even a con-
tinuous washout of HNO3 could not lower the ratio enough to
be in agreement with observations. In this paper we want to
address the question of how useful the HNO3/NOx ratio is for
testing the chemistry in global chemistry transport models,
and how sensitive this ratio is to the parameterization of
washout.
2. Description of the Model
2.1.
IMAGES
The IMAGES model [Miiller and Brasseur, 1995; Pham et
al., 1995; Granier et al., 1996, 1999a, 2000a, 2000b] is a
three-dimensional global chemistry transport model that cal-
culates monthly averaged distributions of some 60 species. IT
resolution of 5 ø x 5 ø and includes 25 levels in
has a horizontal
the vertical extending from the surface to 50 hPa (about 22
km). The dynamics is governed by prescribed monthly aver-
aged transport and temperature fields. The chemical scheme
in the model is adapted from Mtiller and Brasseur [ 1995], and
its current version is given by Granier et al. [2000b]. The
chemistry includes an explicit description of nonmethane
hydrocarbons chemistry including the formation of peroxy-
acetyl nitrate (PAN), important for the transport of reactive
nitrogen from source regions to the free troposphere [Moxim
et al., 1996; Horowitz and Jacob, 1999]. The reaction rates
are from DeMote et al. [1997]. The anthropogenic emissions
are from the EDGAR emission database [Olivier et al., 1996]
which is developed in close cooperation with the Global
Emissions Inventory Activity (GEIA) of the International
Global Atmospheric Chemistry/International Geosphere-Bio-
sphere Programme (IGAC/IGBP) [see also Granier et al.,
2000b]. These emissions are specified on a 1 ø x 1 ø global
resolution for CH4, NOx, CO, and nonmethane hydrocarbons.
They include emissions from fossil fuel use (industry, trans-
port, fuel production, and transmission), biofuel combustion,
industrial processes and solvent use, waste treatment, and
agricultural waste burning. A seasonal variation is imposed on
the anthropogenic emissions according to Miiller [1992].
Biomass burning emissions are taken from Granier et al.
[2000a], based on inventories by Hao and Liu [ 1994], and the
natural emissions are based on Miiller [1992]. Washout is
applied in the model to HNO3, H202, HNO4, CH20, and
several hydrocarbons (CH3COCHO, CH.(cid:127)OOH, C2HsOOH,
C3H7OOH, CH3COCH2OOH, C3H6OHOOH, CH3COOOH)
which are produced by the oxidation of methane, ethane,
propane, propylene, ethylene, isoprene and terpenes. The
washout rate for these species is parameterized on the basis of
the International Satellite Cloud Climatology Project (ISCCP)
monthly averaged distribution and on climatological precipi-
tation rates. IT is applied as a continuous removal rate, with a
magnitude varying as a function of location and month. This
simple parameterization does not account for the sporadic
nature if rain. The production of nitric oxide by lightning dis-
charges is distributed according to the flash frequencies ob-
tained from satellite measurements as reported by Turman
and Edgar [1982]. Total emission of NO from lightning is
assumed to be 5 Tg N/yr. The IMAGES model does not
require a large amount of computer time, and is therefore
suitable for sensitivity studies.

exchanges, and wet scavenging of long-lived chemical species
are prescribed and taken from the output of the middle atmos-
phere version of the National Center of Atmospheric
Research (NCAR) community climate model CCM3 and are
updated every 6 hours. The chemical scheme in the model is
the same as the one used in IMAGES [Granier et al., 2000b],
with reaction rates from DeMote et al. [1997]. The emissions
of CH4, CO, NOx, and hydrocarbons are the same as those
used in IMAGES (see section 2.1). The production of NO by
lightning is distributed as a function of space and season
according to the location of convective clouds as provided by
the general circulation model, and the height of the top of
convective clouds, as formulated by Price and Rind [1992].
As for the IMAGES model, the global production of NO from
lightning is assumed to be 5 Tg N/yr.
The washout processes in MOZART are parameterized as
described by Brasseur et al. [1998]. Washout is represented
as a first-order loss process. The distributions of rainwater
tendency associated with convective and nonconvective pre-
cipitation are provided by CCM3. This approach accounts for
under-cloud scavenging, but ignores the evaporation of rain-
drops along their falling path. The MOZART model requires,
opposite to the IMAGES model, a large amount of computer
time and cannot be used systematically for sensitivity studies.

In order to assess how large the effects are on the whole
troposphere if changing the washout parameterization or in-

IMAGES for the different sensitivity studies discussed here.
The original parameterizations in the model yield a lifetime of
methane of 7.2 years. This is somewhat lower than the life-
time reported by World Meteorological Organization [1999]
of 9.3 years, but that does not effect our comparison since we
are here only interested in changes in the lifetime. If different
parameterizations or reactions cause large changes in this
lifetime, they may be considered too unrealistic to be included
in the model. Applying a continuous washout of 25% to
200% affects the methane lifetime only slightly (-14% to
+4%) as do the different intermittent washout parameteriza-

changes, but using a larger reaction rate for this conversion
(conversion time of 0.6 days, NH, 0-5 km) decreases the
methane lifetime by 24%. A heterogeneous conversion
smaller than 2 days seems therefore not realistic.
4. Conclusions
Observations show that the HNO3/NOx ratio in the free tro-
posphere is about 4+3. Calculations with the IMAGES model
are in good agreement with these observations, but the
MOZART model produces too high ratios (about 7-20). The
good agreement between the original IMAGES calculation
and the observations for the HNO3/NO,(cid:127) ratio might be
fortuitous. Washout in IMAGES is applied as a continuous
removal rate, of which the magnitude varies per location and
month. This parameterization is too simple considering the
sporadic nature of rain. We have shown that the HNO3/NOx
ratio in the IMAGES model is sensitive to the parameteriza-
tion of the washout. Applying different magnitudes of a con-
tinuous washout or different intermittent washout parameter-
izations can increase the HNO3/NOx ratio by about a factor of
2-3. Smaller, but significant, changes of the order ot (cid:127)u-zu(cid:127)o
can be seen in NO, NO2, and ozone as a result of changes in
the washout parameterization. Applying an intermittent wash-
out worsens the agreement with observations, so other effects
might have to be taken into account in models for obtaining
agreement with observations. The heterogeneous conversion
of HNO3 on SO4 (used as a proxy for soot) yields only a small
improvement in the HNO3fNO(cid:127) ratio for most remote loca-
tions. Larger effects of this heterogeneous conversion are
expected over and downwind of polluted locations (e.g.,
eastern China, eastern Europe and Siberia).

eterization of washout, this ratio may be more suited to test
