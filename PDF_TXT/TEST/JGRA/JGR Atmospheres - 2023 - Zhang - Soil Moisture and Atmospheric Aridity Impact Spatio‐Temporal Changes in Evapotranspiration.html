Abstract Evapotranspiration (ET) constitutes the water exchange from land to the atmosphere, which in
turn modulates precipitation and soil moisture (SM). Multiple lines of evidence document complex feedbacks
between changes in ET and temperature, atmospheric CO2 and vegetation greening. However, the existing
analyses on global changes in ET do not account for the direct effects of SM supply and atmospheric water
demand, expressed by vapor pressure deficit (VPD), while considering multiple environmental variables. Here
we evaluated the performance of ET products using 140 flux towers included in the FLUXNET database. All
ET products show reasonable performance, with an overall correlation higher than 0.7 and better performance
at a higher latitude. From analysis of the ensemble mean of annual ET, we show insignificant (P = 0.06) trends
in global ET during 1982–2020 and a significantly (P < 0.01) increasing trend during 2002–2020. Changes in
GLEAM ET generally exert a positive response to changes in SM and a negative response to changes in VPD.
Yet, these effects are not globally consistent and are largely determined by changes in vegetation transpiration.
Using our finding as a benchmark, Earth System Models mostly reproduce the positive response of ET to SM
with less coupling strength, while showing negative effects of VPD on ET with stronger coupling strength.
Our study highlights that concurrent soil drying and atmospheric aridity could intensify water exchanges and
the importance of realistically representing SM-VPD-ET interactions in models for accurate predictions of the
hydrological cycle.
Plain Language Summary Evapotranspiration (ET) links the water exchange between land and
the atmosphere, and thereby plays an important role in regulating precipitation and soil moisture (SM). In this
study, using FLUXNET in situ observations we demonstrate the high performance of different ET products
(GLEAM, ERA5, GLDAS, and MERRA2) which are widely used to characterize long-term changes in global
ET. Decreases in SM and increases in vapor pressure deficit (VPD) are expected to lead to decreases in ET
worldwide. Using our observations as a benchmark, most of Earth System Models participating in the Coupled
Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6 underestimate the positive response of ET to SM, while overestimating
the negative effects of VPD on ET. This study provides a comprehensive understanding of the impacts of SM
and VPD on global changes in ET.
1. Introduction
Evapotranspiration (ET) plays an essential role in transferring water and energy between land and the atmosphere,
and, ranked after precipitation, ET is the second-largest component of the terrestrial water cycle (Trenberth
et al., 2007). Changes in ET affect not only precipitation and land surface water availability, but also the sensi-
ble heat flux by altering land surface temperature (Wang & Dickinson, 2012). Transpiration affects photosyn-
thesis (Wang & Dickinson, 2012) and thus the variations of ET indirectly regulate the terrestrial carbon cycle
(Biederman et al., 2017).
Several studies have investigated ET trends at a global scale, but the magnitude and direction of reported trends
varied in space and time. Moreover, these findings are generally associated with large uncertainties due to vari-
ous data sources used (Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2016). The overall trends (increasing or decreasing) in
global ET since the 1980s are inconclusive across these studies (Liu, Zhang, et al., 2021; Miralles et al., 2014;

Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2016), with a decrease in global ET during 1998–2008 (Jung et al., 2010) and an
increasing trend for the period 1982–2011 (Pan et al., 2020) being reported in recent analyses. Changes in ET can
be controlled by the available water in the soil (Jung et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2016), anthropogenic influences
(Douville et al., 2013; Sterling et al., 2013), climate variability (i.e., El Niño–Southern Oscillation) (Miralles
et al., 2014), increasing temperature (Wang et al., 2022), as well as vegetation greening that controls transpiration
(Wang et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2016). Moreover, changes in vapor pressure deficit (VPD)
and wind speed can affect ET (Zeng et al., 2012). In particular, increasing VPD induces an atmospheric demand
for vapor that leads to increases in ET, while causing stomata closure for further increasing VPD that leads to
decreases in ET (Massmann et al., 2019). Moreover, increasing atmospheric CO2 could reduce ET by decreas-
ing leaf stomatal conductance (Keenan et al., 2013), which is countered by increasing leaf area at the canopy
level (Zhu et al., 2016). Among these drivers, soil moisture (SM) (water supply) and VPD (water demand) exert
direct effects on ET. However, the respective controls of SM and VPD on ET are largely unknown. Covarying
forcing variables, such as temperature, precipitation or vegetation status, are strongly intertwined and cannot be
considered separately, yet only a limited number of variables are typically taken into account in previous studies
(Douville et al., 2013; Jung et al., 2010; Miralles et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2016). Anchored in a process-based
understanding, ESMs can simulate the water cycle response to climate change and enable projections of future
changes in the global water cycle (Pan et al., 2015). An accurate response of ET to changes in forcing variables
is critical for a better understanding of interactions between climate and hydrology. This research area is however
currently not fully explored.
In this study, we aim to provide a comprehensive analysis of SM and VPD and their impacts on ET. We first
analyzed trends in ET during 1982–2020 using multiple state-of-the-art ET products. We then assessed the impacts
of SM and VPD and other environmental variables (i.e., temperature, precipitation, solar radiation, runoff, wind
speed, leaf area index, short vegetation cover, tree cover) on the interannual variability of ET. Finally, by using
our finding as a benchmark, we diagnosed the simulated relationships between ET and SM/VPD from 40 ESMs
included in the sixth Coupled Model Intercomparison Project, Phase (CMIP6).
2. Data and Methods
2.1. Date
2.1.1. Global Land Evaporation Amsterdam Model Version 3
We extracted ET and SM data for 1982–2020 from the Global Land Evaporation Amsterdam Model version
3 (GLEAM-v3) (Martens et  al.,  2017). GLEAM-v3 estimates the actual evaporation by multiplying potential
evaporation with an evaporative stress factor. Potential evaporation is calculated based on the Priestley and Taylor
equation (Priestley & Taylor, 1972) using observations of surface net radiation and near-surface air temperature.
The evaporative stress factor is calculated as a function of microwave vegetation optical depth and root-zone SM.
The latter is calculated using a multi-layer soil water balance that describes the infiltration of observed precipita-
tion through the soil profile. Canopy interception loss is calculated separately in GLEAM using a Gash analytical
model (Martens et al., 2017). The monthly ET and root-zone SM data are provided with a spatial resolution of
0.25° and temporal coverage of 1980–2020. In this study, we also used soil evaporation, transpiration and canopy
interception from GLEAM-v3.
2.1.2. Other ET Products
In total we extracted five other global ET products (Table  1) from MOD16A3, the fifth-generation ECMWF
Re-Analysis (ERA5) (Hersbach et al., 2020), the second Modern-Era Retrospective analysis for Research and
Applications (MERRA2) (Gelaro et al., 2017) and Global Land Data Assimilation System ET (GLDAS) (Rodell
et  al.,  2004). In addition, we processed a data-driven ET product with a relatively short-term record, namely
FLUXCOM (2001–2013) (Jung et al., 2010) which is generated using a machine-learning approach integrating
satellite remote sensing and surface meteorological data. These five ET products are generated using independent
systems and algorithms and their accuracies have been evaluated in multiple studies (Lu et al., 2021; Martens
et al., 2017). All ET products were resampled to a spatial resolution of 0.25° using bilinear interpolation.

2.1.4. VPD
VPD was calculated as the difference between saturated water vapor pressure and actual water vapor pressure.
Saturated water vapor pressure was determined by near-surface (2 m) temperature and actual water vapor pressure
was determined by dew-point temperature. Air temperature and dew-point temperature from ERA5 were used to
calculate VPD.
2.1.5. Environmental Data
We analyzed the impacts of covarying environmental variables on ET. For this we extracted data on air tempera-
ture, precipitation, shortwave radiation and wind speed, obtained from ERA5. The impacts related to vegetation
conditions were also considered by extracting the leaf area index (LAI) during the growing season from the Global
Inventory Monitoring and Modeling System (GIMMS) LAI3g data set. We averaged LAI during the growing
season determined by the monthly climatology temperature above 0°C for each grid cell of the global vegetated
area. Moreover, we extracted MODIS Vegetation Continuous Fields products to derive a percentage of tree cover,
short vegetation cover and bare ground. These variables are based on the Advanced Very High-Resolution Radi-
ometer and are available for 1982–2016. We extracted Köppen-Geiger climate classification data to indicate
different climatic zones (Peel et al., 2007).
2.1.6. CMIP6 Outputs
We extracted monthly ET, SM, air temperature, precipitation and solar radiation for 1982–2014 from 40 ESMs
included in CMIP6 (Table S1 in Supporting Information  S1). VPD was calculated using air temperature and
relative humidity. Solar net radiation was calculated based on the differences between surface downwelling short-
wave radiation and surface upwelling shortwave radiation. For our CMIP6 analysis, a subset of three covarying
variables were selected, namely temperature, precipitation and solar radiation. The remaining variables are not
commonly available across the ESMs. The monthly data were resampled to 1° using bilinear interpolation and
then aggregated to an annual scale (i.e., average monthly temperature and solar radiation, sum monthly precipi-
tation) for further analysis.
2.2. Methodology
2.2.1. ET Evaluation
We first validated the long-term records of various ET products GLEAM, ERA5, GLDAS, and MERRA2 using
the FLUXNET data. In order to obtain relevant observations, we excluded FLUXNET flux towers with ET
records less than 2 years. In total, 140 towers with in situ measurements of ET were kept. Considering the limited
overlapping times available between in situ observations and these ET products, we used monthly observations to
derive robust Pearson's correlation coefficients between them, which were used to assess the performance of the
reanalysis ET products. ET products of FLUXCOM and MOD16A3 were not included due to their shorter period
of observations (dating back to 2001).

2.2.2. Respective Effect Analysis
To explore the respective effects of SM and VPD on ET, we first used SM to bin ET at a pixel level, in which ET
corresponding to high VPD (75–100th percentiles) and low VPD (0–25th percentiles) were extracted to assess
the changes in ET at high VPD and low VPD levels as a function of SM. Correspondingly, changes in ET at high
moisture and low moisture levels were assessed as a function of VPD. Moreover, we used bivariate copulas to
assess the co-occurrence probability (or joint probability) of annual SM and VPD anomalies at 10 × 10 percentile
bins (Zhou et al., 2019; Zscheischler & Seneviratne, 2017). Following Liu et al. (2020) and Dang et al. (2022), the
mean values at the 10 × 10 percentile bins can be used to quantify the respective effects of SM and VPD on ET.
This binning method was suggested to be able to decouple the two variables of SM and VPD (Dang et al., 2022;
Liu et al., 2020). Data were excluded from analysis if the number of data observations were less than 10 in a
given bin.
To better understand the independent effects of SM and VPD on ET in the spatial domain, we used the partial
Spearman's rank correlation to quantify the respective impacts of SM and VPD on ET at the pixel level during
1982–2016. This was done by also taking into account the covarying environmental variables, including air
temperature, precipitation, shortwave radiation, wind speed, leaf area index, tree cover, and short vegetation
cover. Partial Spearman's rank correlation coefficient is a nonparametric measure of the strength and direction
of association that exists between two variables, after controlling for a set of relevant variables. This method
does not require that the measurement variables follow a normal distribution. We further detected the respective
impacts of SM and VPD on GLEAM based transpiration, evaporation and canopy interception using partial
correlations. The independent ET from ERA5 was used to test the robustness of their respective impacts.
We conducted the partial correlation analysis based on the outputs of CMIP6 for the period of 1982–2014, and
here only the covarying variables temperature, precipitation and solar radiation were considered. These varia-
bles were linearly detrended before calculating partial correlation coefficients and significant correlations were
assessed at the 5% significance level (P < 0.05). Partial correlations were calculated for each model of the 40
ESMs (Table S1 in Supporting Information S1) and the multi-model mean correlations of the 40 ESMs were
calculated for further analysis. The partial correlation coefficients were grouped using different climatic zones
(Figure S1 in Supporting Information S1) based on Peel et al. (2007). For the purpose of comparison, we calcu-
lated partial correlation analogously based on GLEAM ET.
2.2.3. Trend Analysis
The Theil-Sen estimator was used to quantify trends in annual ET, SM, and VPD during 1982–2020, and the
Mann-Kendall test was used to determine trends at the 1% significance level (P < 0.01) which is supposed to
retain robust decreasing or increasing trends. The three variables were normalized by the z-score: Xi = (xi – x)/σ(x)
(where x and σ(x) are the mean and standard deviation of xi) before performing trend analysis to enable these
changes to be comparable.
3. Results
3.1. Validation of ET
Monthly ET derived from GLEAM, ERA5, GLDAS, and MERRA2 were validated using FLUXNET-based ET.
Overall, the highest correlation was observed between ERA5 and FLUXNET ET (r = 0.75), followed by GLEAM
(r  =  0.73), GLDAS (r  =  0.72) and MERRA2 (r  =  0.71) (Figures  1a–1c). Using FLUXNET observations as
benchmark, GLEAM and MERRA2 show a slight overestimation of ET (Figures 1a and 1d), while ERA5 and
GLDAS show an overestimation of lower ET and an underestimation of higher ET (Figures 1b and 1c). Spatially,
higher correlations between these ET products and FLUXNET ET were observed across most stations in northern
America, Europe, China and Australia (Figures 1e–1h), including some stations with lower correlations. Lower
correlations were generally observed in the regions of low latitudes (Figures 1e–1h).

the partial Spearman correlation. Overall, strong positive correlations between ET and SM were observed in

and polar biomes. Wind speed also shows negative impacts on ET in all climatic zones except for the polar

from SM (positive effect) and VPD (negative effect) on ET (Figure 4a). It should also be noted that VPD exerts
negative impacts on ET independent of temperature (Figure S3 in Supporting Information S1), though changes

with increasing SM when SM was lower than 0.43  m 3  m −3, beyond which ET decreased with SM increase

3.5. Impacts of Soil Moisture and VPD on ET in CMIP6
We analyzed the simulated relationships between historical CMIP6 ET and SM/VPD with respect to variability
in precipitation, temperature, and solar radiation for 40 ESMs in CMIP6. Overall, ESMs mostly reproduce the
positive correlations between SM and ET (Figure 7a). Still, the magnitude of the multi-model mean correlations
coefficient was lower than the correlations between SM and ET derived from GLEAM data (Figure 8), suggest-
ing an underestimation of ET response to SM in the ESMs. Negative correlations between CMIP6 VPD and ET
were observed in the tropical, arid, and temperate biomes, while positive correlations were found for boreal and
polar biomes. This is in agreement with the identified correlations between ET and ERA5 VPD (Figure 4). The
multi-model mean correlations between CMIP6 ET and VPD were higher than the correlations between ET and
VPD, indicating ESMs overestimate the CMIP6 ET response to VPD. The spatial patterns of ESM generated
correlations between CMIP6 based SM, VPD, and ET were similar to those based on ET, SM, and ERA5 VPD.
However, a disagreement in the magnitude of correlations across the globe was identified (Figures 7b and 7c).
Moreover, we found that the average correlation coefficients between CMIP6 SM and ET, or VPD and ET varied
across models (Figure  8), but most ESMs showed good agreement in the spatial patterns of the correlations
(Figures S6 and S7 in Supporting Information S1).

Thus, this constraint of SM on ET can lead to decreased trends in ET alongside an overall drying of soils (Jung
et al., 2010). However, the negative response on ET with high SM observed at high latitudes is probably due to
abundant precipitation reducing the energy required for ET and the limited access of vegetation to radiation in
these regions of radiation-constrained growth (Dorigo et al., 2012). The widespread negative impacts of VPD
on ET suggest the dominant role of VPD in reducing the stomatal conductance of vegetation to prevent excess
water loss by transpiration (Massmann et al., 2019). This process can potentially lead to decreases in ET given
the predominant role of transpiration in determining changes in ET (Lemordant et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2016).
Moreover, high VPD can accelerate evaporation and reduce SM, strengthening its negative impact on ET. On the
contrary, low VPD occurred at high latitudes, where it is positively related to ET as VPD can increase air demand
of vapor that promotes increases in ET. Additionally, the analysis using independent data from ERA5 and most
ESMs confirmed the robustness of the respective impacts and the observed spatial patterns.
Most ESMs was found to realistically reproduce the strong coupling between ET and SM/VPD. Despite the large
agreement in the spatial patterns of outputs at the global scale, the coupling strength was observed to vary considera-
bly across ESMs. The overall underestimation of modeled ET response to SM can be attributed to the fact that ESMs
account for the positive response of water use efficiency of vegetation to increasing atmospheric CO2 concentration
(Lemordant et al., 2018). The vegetation-CO2 response reduces leaf-level transpiration, resulting in a decrease in the
coupling between SM and ET. However, these “offline” simulations do not capture the effect of CO2 on ET (Lemordant
et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2020). Moreover, the underestimation of the ratio of plant transpiration to total ET in ESMs
(Lian et al., 2018) can also contribute to the underestimation of the coupling between SM and ET, given that transpira-
tion dominates the depletion of SM (Lian et al., 2020). This underestimation was attributed to an inaccurate representa-
tion of canopy light use, canopy interception loss and root water uptake processes in the ESMs (Lian et al., 2018). On
the other hand, the elevated CO2 might also mitigate the VPD-induced stomatal limitation by enhancing water use
efficiency at the leaf level (Dusenge et al., 2019). Yet, this effect could be counteracted at the canopy scale of wide-
spread increase in leaf area (Zhu et al., 2016), leading to the overestimation of modeled ET response to VPD. Finally,
the discrepancies in the response among ESMs can be attributed to the diverse parameterizations of surface energy and
water processes from alternative strands of development of ESMs. Collectively, such bias of ET response to SM and
VPD in ESMs can ultimately lead to uncertainties in the simulations of the terrestrial water cycle and surface energy
balance under future conditions of climate change. A better understanding of the processes representing SM-VPD-ET
interactions in ESMs is required to improve the reliability of projections of future changes in the hydrological cycle.
In addition, this study has important implications for future changes in the interactions of SM-VPD-ET and
how the interactions will respond to climate change. For example, the VPD has a positive effect on ET in polar
and boreal regions where ET is energy limited, but negative in tropical and arid areas. However, shifts in these
relationships are expected, as warming and drying may shift the dominant control of drivers on ecosystem water
fluxes (Denissen et al., 2022; Novick et al., 2016). This is particularly plausible with respect to the hypothesis of
“dry gets drier, wet gets wetter” (Chou et al., 2013; Feng & Zhang, 2015).
While we observed a reasonable performance of the incorporated ET products against in situ observations from
FLUXNET database, some uncertainties may still exist in our analysis and our understanding of trends in ET
or the established relationships between ET and SM/VPD. A relatively large uncertainty in ET products was
observed in regions of low latitudes mainly covering the tropics as compared to regions of high latitudes (from
approximately north of 30°N), suggesting that more empirical research is needed to confirm the effects of SM
and VPD on ET in these regions. Moreover, the scale mismatch between flux tower sites and gridded pixels at
0.25° may contribute to this uncertainty, because the footprint of in situ observations is usually limited to a few
kilometers for the site of the flux tower (Wang et al., 2015). This uncertainty can also be related to differences in
land cover types (Zhu et al., 2022). Additionally, various spatial resolution of ESMs may cause under or overes-
timation in the responses of ET to SM and VPD.
5. Conclusion
Our study provides a point-scale evaluation on various state-of-the-art ET products (i.e., ERA5, GLEAM,
GLDAS, and MERRA2) and show reasonable performance for these ET products. Using these multiple ET
datasets, we documented an insignificant trend (P = 0.06) in global ET during 1982–2020, but a significantly
(P < 0.01) increasing trend in ET for the period 2002–2020. Positive correlations between SM and ET dominated
the terrestrial ecosystems, except at high latitudes where SM was found to be negatively correlated with ET. VPD
