Nonlinear forcing of climate on mountain
denudation during glaciations
Apolline Mariotti 
Stéphane Molliex1,4, Didier L. Bourlès5, Georges Aumaître5 and Karim Keddadouche5
 1 ✉, Pierre-Henri Blard1,2, Julien Charreau1, Samuel Toucanne3, Stephan J. Jorry3,
Denudation is one of the main processes that shapes landscapes. Because temperature, precipitation and glacial extents are key
factors involved in denudation, climatic fluctuations are thought to exert a strong control on this parameter over geological tim-
escales. However, the direct impacts of climatic variations on denudation remain controversial, particularly those involving the
Quaternary glacial cycles in mountain environments. Here we measure in situ cosmogenic 10Be concentration in quartz in marine
turbidites of two high-resolution cores collected in the Mediterranean Sea, providing a near-continuous (temporal resolution
of ~1–2 kyr) reconstruction of denudation in the Southern Alps since 75 kyr ago (ka). This high-resolution palaeo-denudation
record can be compared with well-constrained climatic variations over the last glacial cycle. Our results indicate that total
denudation rates were approximately two times higher than present during the Last Glacial Maximum (26.5–19 ka), the glacial
component of the denudation rates being 1:5þ
 mm yr−1. However, during moderately glaciated times (74–29 ka), denudation

rates were similar to those today (0.24 ± 0.04 mm yr−1). This suggests a nonlinear forcing of climate on denudation, mainly
controlled by the interplay between glacier velocity and basin topography. Hence, the onset of Quaternary glaciations, 2.6 mil-
lion years ago, did not necessarily induce a synchronous global denudation pulse.

How climate and climatic fluctuations affect denudation rates
over geological timescales remains subject to debate1,2. By
relying on sedimentation records3 and thermochronology4,5,
several authors have reported increased denudation rates at the
onset of Pleistocene glaciations around 2.6 Ma (Ma, million years
ago)6, either due to increased variability of Quaternary climatic
cycles3 or the growth of glaciers in mountain ranges, an efficient
agent of erosion4,7. However, marine 10Be/9Be records indicate stable
weathering rates over the same period1. Other studies have high-
lighted the potential biases inherent in sedimentary records8 and
thermochronology9, and the link between weathering and erosion
has also been questioned10. Moreover, the presence of glaciers does
not necessarily lead to an increase in denudation rates11.
In situ terrestrial cosmogenic nuclides (TCNs) such as 10Be record
denudation rates in modern river systems12–14 as well as over geologi-
cal (10 Myr) timescales15,16. TCNs are not affected by the same biases
as sedimentation rate estimates or thermochronology, and they
track both weathering and erosion rates—that is, denudation12–14—
at sufficient resolution (<1 kyr in better cases) to capture the impact
of millennial climatic variations. Previous palaeo-denudation
studies have used continental sedimentary archives such as cave
sediments17,18, terrace deposits19,20, lake sediments21,22 and molasse
deposits in foreland basins15,16. However, these records are often
too discontinuous to accurately reflect the impact of short-term
climatic variations on denudation. Additionally, the most complete
records have been obtained in active tectonic settings over long tim-
escales (>5 Myr), making it difficult to decipher any climatic forc-
ing15. Continuous, well-dated and high-resolution marine sediment
records in well-constrained sediment routing systems have great
potential to overcome these limitations, and they may represent the
gold standard of TCN archives of palaeo-denudation rates.
In this study, we measure in situ 10Be concentrations in 75–0 ka
marine sediments exported by the Var River, which drains the
Southern French Alps into the Mediterranean Sea (Fig. 1).
Southern Alps, the reactive Var Basin and samples
The Southern French Alps form a mountainous region that is
considered to have been tectonically stable since 75 ka, with near
null horizontal and vertical motion23. The Var River catchment
(2,800 km2, maximum altitude of 3,143 m above sea level, mean
altitude of 1,250 m) is a reactive watershed of this massif24, that is,
one that responds quickly to any change in denudation or sedi-
ment transport, with steep slopes (mean 23°) and a small alluvial
plain (<50 km2) that limits on-land sediment storage25. The geol-
ogy of the Var Basin includes two main regions: the crystalline
Mercantour-Argentera massif in the northern and highest part
of the catchment and sedimentary terrains in the southern part.
Quartz-bearing rocks (10Be being measured in quartz) cover 26%
of the Var watershed and are mainly present in the upper part of
the catchment. The present-day 10Be-derived denudation rate of the
entire Var catchment is 0.24 ± 0.04 mm yr−1 (ref. 26). The mouth of
the Var River is connected to a submarine canyon that incises both a
virtually absent continental shelf (width > 200 m)27 and a steep (11°)
continental slope28. Hence, during the Quaternary, sediments drain-
ing the catchment were continuously transferred to the deep depo-
sitional system of the Var in the form of turbidites, building the Var
Sedimentary Ridge (VSR, <2,000 m below sea level)27,28.
Owing to a colder climate than today, the European Alps were
extensively glaciated during the Pleistocene (2.6 Ma to 11.7 ka)29.
In the Var catchment, glaciers occupied the upper valleys of the
Var, Tinée and Vésubie rivers (Fig. 1) during the Last Glacial
Maximum29 (LGM, 26.5–19 ka30). When the glaciers receded and

A total of 26 samples of silts to fine sands (50–250 μm) were col-
lected from two cores drilled in the VSR (Fig. 2). 10Be concentrations
in the samples range from (0.91 ± 0.12) × 104 atoms g−1 (19.8 ka,
LGM) to (5.62 ± 0.44) × 104 atoms g−1 (73.9 ka, Marine Isotopic Stage
(MIS) 5a34) (Supplementary Tables 1 and 2, Methods, Supplementary
Section 1 and Extended Data Fig. 1). Both Dansgaard–Oeschger
stadials and interstadials are documented by our sampling
(Fig. 2a,b). Our data show that grain size has no impact on the
10Be concentrations (Methods and Supplementary Section 2). We
analysed 19 new samples (Methods) to extend the coverage of pre-
existing εNd data from 50–20 ka (ref. 25) to 74–50 ka (Fig. 2c). Our
new εNd values range between −10.3 and −9.6 (average −10.1).
From 10Be concentrations to palaeo-denudation rates
Deriving basin-wide denudation rates from 10Be concentrations
requires computing the 10Be production rate at the time of sediment
production. This parameter is mainly a function of basin altitude,
which has probably remained steady over the last 75 kyr. 10Be pro-
duction is also controlled by glacial extent, because glaciers shield
underlying rocks from cosmic rays, effectively eliminating 10Be pro-
duction beneath several decametres of ice. To test the sensitivity of
the glacial shielding on the denudation rates, we considered two
scenarios to compute palaeo-production rates35. The first assumes
that no glaciers were present between 0 and 75 ka (Scenario 1:
‘Ice-free’). Although this approach contradicts geomorphological
evidence of glaciers in the Var catchment31,36, this endmember sce-
nario provides maximum denudation rate estimates. In the second
scenario (Scenario 2: ‘Glaciated’), we computed palaeo-production
rates by reconstructing glacial extents. We used δ18O data from
planktonic foraminifera24,27 sampled in both cores to compute sea
surface palaeo-temperatures, which we then used to reconstruct the
glacial extents in the Var watershed using a simple two-dimensional
(2D) glacier flow model including a positive degree-day mass bal-
ance37 (details are provided in Supplementary Section 3). The
basin-averaged palaeo-production rates were then calculated with
the BASINGA code, assuming no cosmogenic production under
ice35. Our modelling yields average glacial extents covering 21%,

Potential causes of denudation variations
The coldest event of MIS 4, corresponding to a ~1-kyr-long epi-
sode according to the local δ18O (66–67 ka; Fig. 2b), did not decrease
the 10Be concentration. Additionally, neither climatic and glacial
fluctuations reported in the Alps39,40 during MIS 4–3 nor Atlantic
Dansgaard–Oeschger oscillations (well identified from δ18O records
in the studied cores) had a noticeable impact on the 10Be signal, sug-
gesting that denudation rates are not significantly impacted by this
millennial climatic variability1,33. The observed fluctuations in the
turbidites frequency during MIS 4–3 (Fig. 2d) could thus reflect
changing transport conditions in the sedimentary system rather
than variations in denudation rates25. Nevertheless, εNd data indicate
that partially glaciated valleys (Tinée and Vésubie) represented a
substantial contribution to the sedimentary supply between 75 and
20 ka (MIS 5a–2; Fig. 2c).
Denudation rates obtained using both scenarios during MIS 2
and the LGM were more variable and on average two times higher
than the present-day rate and those during MIS 4–3 (Fig. 2e and
Extended Data Fig. 4). Indeed, the average 10Be concentrations of
samples from the LGM are 50% lower than those of MIS 4–3 and
Holocene samples (Supplementary Table 1). This period of lower
10Be coincides with the largest glacial extent over the last 75 kyr:
during the LGM (19–26.5 ka), glaciers covered nearly 30% of the
quartz-bearing surfaces in the Var catchment (Extended Data
Fig. 3). This drop in concentration could be due to contributions
from 10Be-depleted sediments previously stored in the catchment.
Such a mixing mechanism would lower the average 10Be concen-
tration, thus artificially enhancing denudation rates. A potential
source of 10Be-depleted material is Pliocene sediments stored in
the Var Canyon (mapped as Neogene–Quaternary near the outlet;
Fig. 1), whose incision may have been triggered by ~40 m of sea
level drop between the end of MIS 3 and MIS 241. During warm
ice-free periods, such as the Holocene, the εNd signature of the Var
sediment is low (−11 to −10.5), indicating a lower contribution of
radiogenic highland terrains (Fig. 2)25. Because Pliocene sediments
were deposited during a warm and non-glaciated period, they are
probably also characterized by a low εNd value (−11 to −10.5). Any
reworking of these sediments during sea level drops should thus
be associated with a drop in εNd values. The measured εNd values
during the LGM (mean −10.1) are similar to the values measured
during MIS 4–3, indicating that reworking of Pliocene sediments
is unlikely. Another potential source of 10Be-depleted materi-
als could be palaeo-moraines that accumulated on slopes during
MIS 4–3. Assuming a steady denudation rate of ~0.24 mm yr−1
during the LGM (similar to MIS 4–3 and present-day values26), a
total of ~1 km3 of sediments was produced over this 7.5-kyr-long
period. The same volume of reworked MIS 4–3 moraine sedi-
ments would be required to lower the LGM 10Be concentrations
by 50% (Supplementary Section 4 and Extended Data Figs. 5 and
6). Assuming a simple triangular moraine shape, 1 km3 of MIS 4–3
moraines would have been ~130 km long, 150 m wide and 100 m
high (Supplementary Section 4). Such large moraines are unlikely,
with the largest pre-Holocene moraines observable today in the
Southern Alps being only 25 m high and 80 m wide42. Another
mechanism is therefore required to explain the 50% drop of cos-
mogenic 10Be concentrations observed in Var sediments during
the LGM.
When LGM glaciers intruded into lower valleys, they were prob-
ably able to increase bedrock incision and local denudation rates.
The main mechanism here could be the basal velocity of the gla-
ciers, which controls the denudation efficiency43. By flowing into
narrow valleys, LGM glaciers had higher local velocities that MIS
4–3 glaciers and hence induced higher glacial incision43,44 (Fig. 3).
After a lag time of the few thousand years necessary to remove
the first metres of the 10Be-rich fluvial landscape soil, the glacial
incision then delivered shielded material to the system. After mix-
ing with the fluvial sediments in the lower part of the watershed,
this glacial input induced a drop in 10Be concentrations.
Such denudation solely due to the glacial incision can be quanti-
fied using a mass balance approach, which assumes that the 10Be
concentration measured in each sample is a mix of glacial and flu-
vial contributions45 (Supplementary Section 5). The resulting aver-

During MIS 4–3, between 75 and 26.5 ka, glaciers existed but
were probably restricted most of the time to the highest parts of
the catchment, in cirques and hanging valleys, with a low erosive
power44 (Extended Data Fig. 9). Only for ~1 kyr during MIS 4 did
the glaciers possibly reach the lower valley, as in the LGM (according
to the local δ18O curve; Fig. 2b). However, this glacial advance was
probably too short to critically carve the landscape and deeply erode
the bedrock to produce 10Be-depleted sediments. During the LGM,
the advance of glaciers in deep valleys probably increased denuda-
tion during 7 kyr. Along with the reworking of MIS 4–3 moraines,
this contributed to lowering the 10Be concentrations and increasing
the overall denudation rates of the Var Basin (Figs. 2e and 3).
During deglaciation (19–11 ka)36, the overall 10Be-denudation
rates dropped, becoming close to the present-day value (Fig. 2e). The
denudation decrease between 19 and 15 ka is concomitant with the
εNd-inferred shift of sediment sources from exposed crystalline mas-
sifs to sedimentary cover (Fig. 2c), and with a decrease in riverine
sediment inputs (Fig. 2d)24. These changes suggest that denudation
dynamics returned to fluvial conditions during that time25. At 10 ka,
we observe a spike in denudation rates synchronous with a positive
εNd excursion (Fig. 2e). This short-term 10Be spike is only supported
by one core sample and occurred during a period of increased rain-
fall in the Mediterranean region46. Together with the εNd spike, this
excursion is characteristic of a paraglacial lag in the sedimentary
transport of previously eroded glacial sediments47 and should not
be considered an actual increase in bedrock denudation25. After this
spike, Holocene denudation rates are similar to present-day rates
and those observed during MIS 4–3 (Figs. 2 and 3).
Our 75-kyr-long denudation record from the Southern French
Alps indicates a nonlinear link between climate and denudation, as
shown by the relationship between δ18O-derived sea surface tem-
peratures and 10Be-derived denudation rates (Fig. 4).

implications for other regions and climatic periods
Our data suggest that climatic variations may only strongly affect
denudation beyond a certain threshold, probably controlled by
the glacier dynamics43,44, the duration of glacial advances and
temperature-driven processes (for example, frost cracking)48,49.
Because the denudation is enhanced during the LGM period, when
glaciers advanced and carved deep U-shaped narrow valleys, basal
ice velocity was probably the main driver of this nonlinear response.
This mechanism was suggested for present-day43 and million years
timescales18, and our study detects such behaviour at the glacial–
interglacial timescale.
In conclusion, our study indicates that the denudation response
to Quaternary glaciations is complex and nonlinear in glaciated
areas. This result has strong and broad implications for the erosion

Methods
Cores and samples. We sampled sediments (Supplementary Table 1) from two
Calypso piston cores collected in 200852 (Fig. 1): ESSK08-CS01 (22 m long) is from
the VSR levee crest (2,146 m water depth) and ESSK01-CS13 (24 m long) is from the
southwestern flank of the VSR53 (2,473 m water depth). Age models for both cores
were previously anchored by 25 14C dates, as well as high-resolution planktonic
oxygen isotope stratigraphy24,27 (Fig. 2a,b and Supplementary Table 2). Accordingly,
core ESSK08-CS01 spans 0.3–29.8 ka and ESSK08-CS13 spans 0.5–74.2 ka,
corresponding to mean sedimentation rates of 80 and 30 cm kyr−1, respectively.
Twenty-six samples (19 in ESSK01-CS13 and four in ESSK08-CS01) were
selected from coarser (>50 µm) turbiditic facies suitable for in situ 10Be analyses in
quartz26. Because of the reactive nature of the watershed and the depth of the VSR,
all samples were buried quickly and have not been re-exposed to cosmic radiation:
changes in 10Be concentrations thus reflect only denudation rate variations. Sample
preparation and analytical methods are described in the Supplementary Information.

Quartz-free areas (based on 1:250,000-scale French Geological Survey geological
maps58,59) were excluded from the basin-averaged production rate calculations.
Production rates were corrected for topographic shielding, ice cover and changes
in palaeomagnetic variations occurring during the integration time35. Topographic
shielding factors were calculated using an ArcGIS toolbox60 that computes both
self-shielding and shading. Ice cover was estimated independently for each sample,
and production rates were considered null under the extent of the ice cover.
Palaeomagnetic changes were averaged over the 3 kyr preceding the age of the sample.
We used palaeomagnetic data from ref. 61 for samples dating to 0–60 ka and data from
ref. 62 for older samples. Palaeomagnetic correction factors ranged from 0.90 to 1.06.
Integration time. A crucial consideration in cosmogenic dating is the method’s
ability to detect changes in denudation rates. This mainly depends on the integration
time, Tint, which is the amount of time required to remove ~60 cm of bedrock,
calculated as Tint = Λ/(ρε), where Λ is the neutronic attenuation length (g cm−2), ρ is
the rock density (g cm−3) and ε is the denudation rate (cm yr−1)12,63. To detect past
changes, this integration time must be shorter than the timescale of denudation rate
changes. In this dataset, the computed integration time ranges from 0.5 to 3.9 kyr.
Impact of grain size on in situ 10Be concentration measurements. Our previous
study showed that, in modern Var sediments, both the 50–100 μm and 100–250 μm
size fractions have similar 10Be concentrations at the river outlet26. To verify that
submarine processes did not induce a grain size-related bias, we analysed 10Be
concentrations in the same size fractions when the sample size was large enough
(17 of 26 samples). 10Be concentrations were compatible at the 2σ level for both
size fractions in all 17 samples, and the best-fit regression is statistically compatible
with the 1:1 line (Extended Data Fig. 1). These results suggest that submarine
turbidite dynamics do not induce a record bias. Consequently, we used the
weighted mean 10Be concentrations from both grain sizes to derive denudation
rates (Supplementary Table 1).
