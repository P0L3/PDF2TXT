Abstract
The Arctic and North Atlantic Oscillations (AO/NAO) are large-scale annual modes of
atmospheric circulation that have shifted in the last 30 years. Recent changes in arctic
climate, including increasing surface air temperature, declining sea ice extent, and shifts
in the amounts seasonality of precipitation are linked to the strong positive phase of the
AO/NAO. Here, we show that phase changes in the AO/NAO are recorded in the isotopic
(d18O and D-carbon isotope discrimination) characteristics of the long-lived circum-arctic
plant, Cassiope tetragona, as summer rain has become a more important water source
than snowmelt water which in turn has lead to decreases in D and reductions in plant
stem growth. These isotopic records in C. tetragona may facilitate reconstructions of
climate, plant–soil water relations, plant gas exchange attributes and a mechanistic
understanding of growth responses to shifts in atmospheric circulation. If plant
specimens were available for populations across the arctic as part of the International
Polar Year, these archives could provide a circum-arctic record of historical climate
change and associated shifts in physiological plant performance and growth.

Introduction
The Arctic and North Atlantic Oscillations (AO/NAO)
are large-scale annual modes of atmospheric circulation
that have shifted in the last 30 years (Thompson &
Wallace, 1998; Thompson et al., 2000). Recent changes in
arctic climate, including increasing surface air tempera-
ture, declining sea ice extent, and changes in the
seasonality and amounts of precipitation are linked to
the positive phase of the AO/NAO (Serreze et al., 2000;
Peterson et al., 2002; Rigor et al., 2002; Stone et al., 2002;
Schaefer et al., 2004). While the effects of AO/NAO
phase changes on terrestrial processes have been
mixed, there does appear to be a suite of consequences
to recent changes in the Arctic including; reduced snow
pack, earlier snow melt, shrub increases, glacial melt-

occurs during transpiration and biosynthesis (Yakir &
Sternberg, 2000). The use of leaf dry matter d18O to
examine changes in plant water sources has the
advantage of being a time-integrated measurement of
plant water sources during a growing season (Yakir &
Sternberg, 2000), as opposed to using extracted xylem
water that is sample date speciﬁc.
In conjunction,
leaf gas exchange responses (as
reﬂected in D-values) to divergent soil/plant water
regimes are manifested during photosynthesis, where-
by, D-values are higher when soil and plant water is
abundant (high rates of stomatal conductance or lower
rates of photosynthesis) and lower when soil and plant
water supplies are low (lower
rates of stomatal
conductance or higher rates of photosynthesis) (Ehler-
inger & Cooper, 1988; Ehleringer et al., 1991; Welker
et al., 1993, 2003, 2004). Previous studies have shown
that D-values of C. tetragona plants are lower when
summer rain is used by these plants as opposed to snow
melt water because these drier conditions (no subsur-
face snow melt water inputs throughout the summer)
result in reductions in stomatal conductance (Welker et
al., 1995). Thus, coisotope analyses of C. tetragona annual
growth increments (AGIs) over long time periods may
provide a retrospective technique to ascertain changes
in plant water sources and shifts in leaf gas exchange
that accompany large-scale climate changes.
C. tetragona is a circum-arctic, evergreen, dwarf-shrub
that produces identiﬁable AGIs, much like tree-rings
(Callaghan et al., 1989; Johnstone & Henry, 1997; Molau,
1997). Using dendrochronological techniques, growth
chronologies of up to 150 years can be constructed from
live stems and used in proxy climate and growth
reconstructions (Callaghan et al., 1989; Havstrom et al.,
1993; Johnstone & Henry, 1997; Rayback & Henry,
2005a, b). Retrospective analysis has shown that C.
tetragona exhibits growth sensitivity to interannual
ﬂuctuations in climate (i.e. temperature, precipitation,
and thawing degree days) (Callaghan et al., 1989), and
the isotopic characteristics (dD or d18O and D) of the
annual growth segments reveal interannual differences
in plant water sources and associated changes in leaf
gas exchange (CO2 and H2O) (Welker et al., 1995). Until
now, it was unclear if changes in the arctic hydrologic
cycle, associated with shifts in the AO/NAO, were
simultaneously manifested in plant source water, leaf
physiology, and growth. If C. tetragona isotopic attri-
butes record shifts in the phases of the AO/NAO, then
plants collected throughout the Arctic could provide a
robust repository of the spatial manifestation of AO/
NAO phase changes and insight as to the mechanistic
processes governing growth responses to changes in
climate.
Methods and materials
Plants were collected in 1998 from two populations
separated by 500 m elevation, at Alexandra Fiord,
Ellesmere Island, Canada (791N, 751W). At each site
(Lowland and Dome), one stem was taken from each of
three individual plants (N 5 3) for growth and isotopic
analysis. Using dendrochronological techniques, AGIs
from live stems were identiﬁed, measured to the
nearest 0.01 mm for length, cross-dated, and severed
(Johnstone & Henry, 1997; Rayback & Henry, 2005a, b).
Isotopic (d18O and d13C) analyses were conducted on
the oven dried AGIs (pooled across the three replicates
for each population because of mass limitations for
analysis) using pyrolysis techniques for d18O (Saurer
et al., 1997; Scheidegger et al., 2000), and combustion
techniques for d13C (Welker et al., 1993, 2004). Carbon
isotope discrimination (D) values were calculated based
on the stem d13C- and d13C-values for air that ranged
from (cid:1)6.78% in 1933 to (cid:1)8.00% in 1996 using ice core
records (Francy et al., 1999). Lower d18O values in
organic matter may reﬂect a higher proportion of
snowmelt water in the soils and the use of snowmelt
water by plants during metabolism (Welker et al., 1995).
Alternatively, enriched d18O values may reﬂect higher
levels of rain water in the soil and an increase in the use
of rain water by plants (Welker et al., 1995; Welker, 2000;
Dawson et al., 2002). Higher values of D reﬂect a greater
degree of stomatal conductance and/or reduced photo-
synthesis, while lower values of D reﬂect reduced
stomatal conductance, often found under conditions of
water shortage (i.e. drought), and/or increased photo-
synthesis (Farquhar et al., 1989; Dawson et al., 2002).

Results and Discussion
The d18O-values of C. tetragona stem segments were
consistently low for plants from the two populations
between 1935 and 1970 (Fig. 1a). However, beginning in
the early 1970s, there was a gradual and deﬁnitive
enrichment in the annual d18O-values of the C. tetragona
stem segments. In the early 1990s,
the trend rose
abruptly, reaching values of (cid:2) 21% at the Dome site
and (cid:2) 24% at the Lowland site. The change in d18O-
values
in the early 1990s corresponds with the
intensifying of AOW index, increasing spring surface
air temperatures, decreasing sea level pressure, and
increasing cyclonic activity at high latitudes (Walsh
et al., 1996; Thompson et al., 2000).
We also found signiﬁcant (Po0.02) positive correla-
tions between the AO/NAO indexes and the d18O-
values of C. tetragona stem segments for both popula-
tions (Fig. 1b, c). The results indicate that during the
predominantly negative phase of the AO/NAO, soil
and plant water sources were dominated by snowmelt
water, while during the recent positive phase (1970–
1996) of the AO/NAO, the annual d18O-values were
enriched, indicative of an increase in the use of rain
water by these plants during the growing season
(Welker et al., 1995; Welker, 2000; Dawson et al., 2002).
In fact, climate data from the Eureka High Arctic
Weather Station, Ellesmere Island, show summer rain-
fall increased during the 1970–1996 period (Fig. 1d) and
corresponds to reductions in snow depth and earlier
snow melt across the Arctic (Dye, 2002; Stone et al.,
2002). These increases in summer precipitation appear
to be associated with low summertime sea level
pressure, increased cyclonic activity, and cloudy, cool,
rainy weather, all linked to the positive phase of the
AO/NAO (Walsh et al., 1996; Serreze et al., 2000;
Thompson et al., 2000; Aanes et al., 2002; Wang & Key,
2003).
The changes in plant water sources (d18O) associated
with the transition in the AO/NAO also corresponded
to shifts in the gas exchange (D) properties of C.
tetragona plants (Fig. 1e). When annual d18O-values of
the C. tetragona segments were enriched, the D-values
were lower. The lower D-values could be indicative of
reduced rates of stomatal conductance and/or in-
creased rates of assimilation. Declining D-values corre-
spond with a trend toward reduced aboveground
growth during the positive phase of the AO/NAO,
suggesting that increasing photosynthesis (because of
increases in diffuse radiation with higher cloud cover-
Roderick et al., 2001) is an unlikely explanation for
observed changes in carbon isotope discrimination (Fig.
1f). Reduced rates of stomatal conductance could also
be associated with cloudy, cool conditions as cold soils

Lower aboveground plant growth under conditions
of higher summer rainfall (summer rainfall is positively
associated with the AOS index; June–August, n 5 27;
r 5 0.393; Po0.05) and is counter
to the classic
assumption that high arctic plants can be water limited
(Bliss, 1977). However, we have found other high arctic
plants do not always respond with greater above-
ground growth when they receive higher amounts of
summer rain which may be associated with increases in
reproductive output or greater rates of root growth as
opposed to increases in segment length or increases in
leaf mass (Welker et al., 1993).
Our discovery illustrates the complex interactions
between climate phases, the hydroecology of arctic
ecosystems, and the physiological ecology of C. tetra-
gona plants (Fig. 2). Concurrent with the recent positive
phase of the AO/NAO, there have been reductions in
winter precipitation and earlier snow melt, increases in
summer rain (cloudy and cool), and increasing reliance
on summer rain by C. tetragona for biosynthesis that has
lead to increases in the d18O-values of annual growth
segments. These changes in water sources correspond
with reductions in carbon isotope discrimination (D),
indicative of reductions in stomatal conductance, and
reductions in stem growth.
These isotopic and growth records in C. tetragona
plants provide a record of changes in the phases of the
AO/NAO and a mechanistic understanding of the
processes controlling C. tetragona growth responses to
the positive phase of the AO (Aanes et al., 2002). If
circum-arctic collections were available as part of the
International Polar Year, one may be able to articulate
whether these shifts in atmospheric circulation are
manifested uniformly across tundra landscapes, or
whether changes in the hydroecology and physiology
tetragona are region speciﬁc. And, because
