Discrepancy of Interdecadal Changes in the Asian Region among the NCEP–NCAR
Reanalysis, Objective Analyses, and Observations
RENGUANG WU AND J. L. KINTER III
Center for Ocean–Land–Atmosphere Studies, Calverton, Maryland
B. P. KIRTMAN
School for Computational Sciences, George Mason University, Fairfax, Virginia, and Center for Ocean–Land–Atmosphere Studies,

ABSTRACT
This study compares decadal means and interdecadal changes of surface and sea level pressures, tropo-
spheric heights, and winds in the National Centers for Environmental Prediction–National Center for
Atmospheric Research (NCEP–NCAR) reanalysis with objective analyses and observations. It is found that
over Asia the NCEP–NCAR reanalysis pressures and heights are systematically lower than objective
analyses and observations before the late 1970s. The magnitude of the differences changes from one decade
to another and shows obvious seasonal dependence. The nonuniform spatial distribution of pressure and
height differences is consistent with the discrepancy in lower-level meridional winds along the east Asian
coast. The seasonal dependence of pressure differences affects the strength of the seasonal cycle over Asia.
More importantly, large changes in the discrepancies from one decade to another lead to inconsistent
interdecadal changes between the reanalysis and objective analyses or observations in the Asian region.
While the reanalysis displays a large increase of pressure around 1977 and in the mid-1960s and an obvious
decrease in the late 1950s, the changes are small in objective analyses and observations. Inconsistent
interdecadal changes are also present in tropospheric heights and winds. The results indicate that the
reanalysis may overestimate interdecadal changes over Asia. This calls for caution in utilizing the reanalysis
output to study the interdecadal variability or the interannual variability without removal of interdecadal
variations in the Asian region.
1. Introduction
The National Centers for Environmental Prediction–
National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCEP–
NCAR) reanalysis project produced a 50-yr analysis of
global atmospheric circulation (Kalnay et al. 1996;
Kistler et al. 2001). Through quality control and assimi-
lation of various observations, the reanalysis reduces
some spurious “climate” shifts that were present in ear-
lier analyses. Because of its multidecade temporal ex-
tent, the reanalysis output provides a potentially useful
dataset for understanding the interdecadal variability
in the atmosphere and ocean. For example, based

NCEP–NCAR reanalysis should be restricted to Aus-
tralia, New Zealand, North America, Asia, and Europe.
The changes of the reanalysis quality due to the ob-
serving system may introduce unrealistic interdecadal
shifts into the reanalysis output. Thus, before utilizing
the reanalysis output to study climate variations on in-
terdecadal time scales, it is necessary to evaluate
whether the interdecadal shifts in the reanalysis are ar-
tificial or realistic. Several studies have indicated that
apparent shifts in the Tropics in the reanalysis may be
unreal (Trenberth et al. 2001; Kinter et al. 2004; Wu
and Xie 2003). In a validation study of temperature
fields from the NCEP–NCAR and the European Cen-
tre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF)
reanalyses using temperatures from the Microwave
Sounding Unit (MSU), Trenberth et al. (2001) identi-
fied two spurious discontinuities in tropical tempera-
tures of the ECMWF reanalysis with jumps to warmer
values throughout the Tropics below 500 hPa in late
1986 and early 1989. Sturaro (2003) identified disconti-
nuities in August–September 1976 and December
1978–January 1979 in the time series of principal com-
ponent analysis applied to the NCEP–NCAR reanalysis
monthly temperature. Kinter et al. (2004) demon-
strated that the divergent circulation regime shift in the
tropical Pacific in the mid-1970s, as revealed by the
reanalysis velocity potential, vertical velocity, and pre-
cipitation, is not found in other decade-long analyses of
tropical precipitation. Wu and Xie (2003) indicated that
the reanalysis surface wind change around 1977 is in
conflict with the Comprehensive Ocean–Atmosphere
Data Set (COADS) wind change. In studying interdec-
adal variations in Northern Hemisphere winter storm-
track intensity, Chang and Fu (2002) pointed out the
possibility that the biases in the reanalysis may have led
to an overestimation of the interdecadal variability.
Somewhat different results were recently reported by
Chelliah and Bell (2004), who indicated that the tropi-
cal multidecadal mode derived from the reanalysis 200-
hPa velocity potential is consistent with that based on
surface temperature and compares favorably with ob-
served station data and published results of decadal
climate variability in the key loading regions, including
the west African monsoon region, the central tropical
Pacific, the Amazon basin, and the tropical Indian
Ocean.
A question to be asked is whether artificial shifts are
present in other regions, for example, Asia. Recently,
Yang et al. (2002) pointed out that the NCEP–NCAR
reanalysis quality in the Asian region may be low prior
to 1968. This could be related to the fact that very little
surface pressure data were assimilated before 1968 be-
cause of the erroneous encoding of surface pressure
data (see http://wwwt.emc.ncep.noaa.gov/gmb/bkistler/
psfc/psfc.html). The December–January–February
(DJF) sea level pressure difference map between the
periods of 1949–67 and 1968–2000 shows substantial
discrepancies in eastern Russia, Mongolia, and north-
ern China between the reanalysis and the Trenberth
and Paolino analysis. The reanalysis sea level pressure
over Asia displays an increase in winter and a decrease
in summer before 1968, indicating a stronger annual
cycle. Yang et al. (2002) also suggested that if the data
quality flaw over Asia is due to the lack of surface
pressure in the reanalysis before 1968, the problem may
be more serious in the lower troposphere than in the
upper troposphere. Recently, Inoue and Matsumoto
(2004) compared the summer sea level pressure change
over east Eurasia from 1960–79 to 1980–99 in the
NCEP–NCAR reanalysis, the 40-yr ECMWF reanalysis
(ERA-40), and the Climatic Research Unit of the Uni-
versity of East Anglia (CRU–UEA) analysis. The sea
level pressure over Mongolia and its vicinity exhibits an
obvious increase in the NCEP–NCAR reanalysis, but a
decrease in the ERA-40 and CRU–UEA analysis. The
sea level pressure averaged over 40°–60°N, 90°–120°E

In this study, we utilize available objective analyses
and observations of surface and sea level pressures, tro-
pospheric heights, and winds and make a detailed com-
parison with the reanalysis over Asia for different pe-
riods and seasons. Large discrepancies are found at the
surface as well as in the mid- and upper troposphere
before the late 1970s between the reanalysis and objec-
tive analyses or observations. Consistent with the non-
uniformity of the pressure and height discrepancies,
meridional wind discrepancies are present along the
East Asian coast. The seasonal dependence of the dis-
crepancies affects the intensity of the seasonal cycle.
The changes of the discrepancies over time introduce
artificial interdecadal changes.

2. Datasets
We use monthly mean surface and sea level pres-
sures; geopotential heights at 1000, 850, 500, and 200
hPa; winds at 850 hPa; and surface temperature from
the NCEP–NCAR reanalysis (Kalnay et al. 1996).
These variables cover the period 1948–2001 and have a
regular resolution of 2.5° latitude and 2.5° longitude
except for surface temperature, which is on T62 Gauss
grids. We also use the observation number counts for
various types of observations that are available on 2.5°
(cid:1) 2.5° grids for the period 1946–98 from the 50-yr
monthly mean CD-ROM (Kistler et al. 2001).
Gridpoint objective analyses obtained from NCAR
are utilized for comparison. These include the Tren-
berth and Paolino sea level pressure (Trenberth and
Paolino 1980; more information available online at
http://dss.ucar.edu/datasets/ds010.1) and tropospheric
analyses of 500- and 200-hPa geopotential heights
(more information available online at http://dss.ucar.
edu/datasets/ds085.1). The Trenberth and Paolino sea
level pressure dataset was compiled from gridded at-
mospheric analyses of various sources (see Table 1 of
Trenberth and Paolino 1980 for details). The tropo-
spheric analyses were computed from various opera-
tional models and meteorological projects, including
U.S. Navy, U.S. Air Force, Earth Science Systems Pro-
gram Office (ESSPO; an interagency project to prepare
1955–60 grids), the National Meteorological Center
(NMC; NCEP after 1994), NCAR, and the Extended
Forecast Division of NMC. The sea level pressure data
start in 1899 and are available north of 15°N. The 500-
hPa data are available north of 20°N for the period
from 1946 to March 2001. The 200-hPa height data are
available north of 20°N for the period from 1963 to
March 2001. All of the above objective analysis data are
monthly means on 5° (cid:1) 5° grids. In constructing maps
of the differences between the reanalysis and objective
analyses, bilinear interpolation is applied to the objec-
tive analyses.
The Trenberth and Paolino sea level pressure data
were subjected to corrections that were made to elimi-
nate errors and remove the discontinuities identified in
association with changes in the data source in the Asian
region (Trenberth and Paolino 1980). This may also
have removed some real trends. Thus, the analyzed sea
level pressure data may not be suitable for determining
long-term trends in the Asian region. Further, because
of changes in the amount of data going into the analysis,
the Trenberth and Paolino dataset could have time-
dependent biases of its own. As such, solely based on
comparison of the reanalysis with the Trenberth and
Paolino data, it could not be determined whether the
reanalysis sea level pressure changes are realistic or
not. Nevertheless, the comparison should be helpful for
revealing where large discrepancies could occur, which
should provide a guide for further checking the reanaly-
sis using available observations.
Monthly mean station pressures from the Global His-
torical Climatology Network (GHCN; Vose et al. 1992)
are used in this study. These data were obtained from
the Web site of the Carbon Dioxide Information
Analysis Center (CDIAC; http://cdiac.ornl.gov/ftp/
ndp041) and are only available up to 1988. In this study,
we only use those stations in China, Korea, and Japan.

b. Spatial structure
To show the spatial structure of the discrepancies
between the analysis and the reanalysis, we display in
Figs. 3 and 4 the distribution of sea level pressure and
500-hPa height differences for four periods: 1978–87,
1967–76, 1957–63, and 1948–55. These four periods are
chosen because the sea level pressure differences show
relatively small variations within these periods but large
changes between these periods (Fig. 2).
At sea level, there are a few regions where large
differences of the same sign are present for all four
periods (Fig. 3). These are the Tibetan Plateau and
Greenland. The large differences in these regions are
likely related to the high topography and the different
methods used to extrapolate to sea level. Differences of
the same sign for the 4 periods are also seen in the
Mexican highlands but with smaller magnitude com-
pared to the Tibetan Plateau and Greenland. There are
no obvious differences over the American and Cana-
dian Rockies probably because the model topography
is lower in this region, and more reliable observations
were assimilated in the reanalysis.
Pronounced differences are seen over northern
China, Mongolia, and eastern Russia where the re-
analysis sea level pressure is lower than the analysis
(Fig. 3). The magnitude of the differences changes from
one period to another. During 1978–87, the differences
are about 1 hPa in the region of 50°–70°N, 100°–160°E
(Fig. 3a). During 1967–76, the differences reach about 5
hPa around 45°N, 105°E (Fig. 3b). During 1957–63, the
differences increase to about 7 hPa at the center (Fig.
3c). During 1948–55, the differences are about 3 hPa
(Fig. 3d). The sea level pressure in the polar region
tends to be lower in the reanalysis than in the analysis
during the reanalysis period. The difference is mostly
around 1 hPa but reaches 2 hPa for the period 1967–76.
Over western Canada, the reanalysis sea level pressure
is lower than the analysis by about 1 hPa before the late
1970s.

4d). The temporal variations of the differences are con-

2. The differences are more widespread than at sea

d. Discussion of plausible reasons
The sea level pressure is not measured but is derived
using extrapolation methods. Different extrapolation
methods are used in the reanalysis and in objective
analyses. For the reanalysis model, the lowest model-
level temperature is used, and the underground tem-
perature is assumed to follow a moist adiabatic lapse
rate. From this, the 1000-hPa (and possibly 500 hPa at
high terrains) height is extrapolated. Then, the sea level
pressure is determined from 1000- and 500-hPa heights.
Thus, the reanalysis sea level pressure and 1000-hPa
height are closely related. For the analyses, the sea level
pressure is derived based on the assumption that the
underground air has the same temperature as the av-
erage of the preceding 12 h of station temperatures
(Glickman 2000). This average station temperature can
differ from the lowest model-level temperature. As
such, the differences in temperature and assumed lapse
rates can contribute to the sea level pressure differ-
ences in regions of high elevations.
While the absolute value of the sea level pressure and
in turn the difference between the reanalysis and the
analysis are related to the surface pressure, tempera-
ture, and approximations used in the extrapolation, the
impacts of the extrapolation methods on the sea level
pressure changes are likely small because the extrapo-
lation method is fixed in time. Thus, the sea level pres-
sure changes are mostly attributed to surface pressure
and temperature changes, and perhaps network
changes. The objective analyses will be more sensitive
to station changes because they used an unrealistic tem-
perature profile for the below-ground temperature.
Here, we compare sea level pressure changes with
those of 1000-hPa height, surface pressure, and surface
temperature in the late 1970s in the reanalysis.
Over east Asia, the increase in sea level pressure is
consistent with that in the 1000-hPa height (Fig. 1b ver-
sus Fig. 6a; here we use 1 hPa (cid:2)8 m according to the
hydrostatic equation). The surface pressure increase
(Fig. 6b) is smaller than the sea level pressure increase
especially over the Tibetan Plateau where the differ-
ences are about 1–2 hPa. In this region, the surface
temperature decreased by about 0.5°–1°C (Fig. 6c). Es-
timation based on the hydrostatic equation shows that
such surface temperature changes can contribute 1–2
hPa to the sea level pressure increase, which accounts
for the difference between the surface and sea level
pressure changes.
Since the reanalysis model and assimilation system
are fixed in time, the temporal changes in the discrep-
ancies between the reanalysis and analyses are ex-
pected to relate to changes in the number and/or qual-
ity of observations assimilated into the reanalysis sys-
tem and changes in the objective analyses. Here, we
examine the temporal evolution of the observation
number counts provided by the CD-ROM in Kistler et
al. (2001). It should be pointed out that the number of
observations in the CD-ROM does not necessarily in-
dicate the number of observations actually assimilated.
The differences depend on the rejection rate of obser-
vations through the reanalysis quality control process.
Thus, our comparison cannot provide any definite re-
lation in the temporal evolution between the discrep-
ancies and the number of observations.

6. Concluding remarks
Based on comparison of variations in surface and sea
level pressures, and tropospheric heights and winds be-
tween the NCEP–NCAR reanalysis and objective
analyses or observations, remarkable discrepancies are
identified over Asia before the late 1970s. The reanaly-
sis pressure is much lower than the analysis or the ob-
served. Large discrepancies extend to the mid- and up-
per troposphere. In association with the spatial nonuni-
formity of pressure and height discrepancies, large
inconsistencies between the reanalysis and objective
analyses or observations occur in lower-level meridi-
onal winds along the east Asian coast. The reanalysis
northerly winds over east Asia are much weaker than in
analyses or observations before the late 1970s.
Because of the seasonal dependence of the discrep-
ancies, the seasonal cycle in the Asian region differs
between the reanalysis and the analyses or observa-
tions, especially in the lower troposphere. In 1978–87,
the reanalysis pressure is lower in winter but higher in
summer than in the analyses or observations. This leads
to a weaker seasonal cycle in the reanalysis compared
to the analyses or observations. The reanalysis seasonal
cycle is also weaker during 1967–76 as a result of larger
differences in winter than in summer. In the periods
before the mid-1960s, the reanalysis seasonal cycle is
stronger than the analyses because the differences in
summer are larger than in winter. The seasonal depen-
dence of the discrepancies is weaker for 500-hPa height.
The temporal fluctuations of the discrepancies on
decadal time scales cause inconsistent interdecadal
changes over Asia between the reanalysis and objective
analyses or observations. The reanalysis usually dis-
plays much larger interdecadal changes in the late
1970s, mid-1960s, and late 1950s. The spatial structure
of the discrepancies also leads to differences in inter-
decadal changes of meridional winds along the east
Asian coast.
Changes in the discrepancy are likely due to changes
in the observational network. However, the changes
may be nonlocal, and the discrepancies are not a linear
function of the number of observations. The possible
effects of incorrect observations cannot be excluded.
These initial errors could change with time and could
be amplified by the reanalysis system. On the other
hand, the consistent fluctuations in sea level pressure
and 500-hPa height discrepancies on decadal time
scales indicate that some dynamical control imposed by
the reanalysis system may play an important role. For
example, the reanalysis displays an enhancement of
downward motion over midlatitude east Asia in the late
1970s. This may contribute to an increase in the height
in both the mid- and lower troposphere with a larger
effect at the surface. The efficiency of the dynamical
control may depend on the season, as suggested by the
seasonality in the discrepancies, especially at the sur-
face.
