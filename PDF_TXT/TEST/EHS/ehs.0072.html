A new orchid species, Bulbophyllum wolongense, from the Wolong National Nature Reserve (Sichuan,
China), is proposed and described. Using the nuclear ribosomal DNA (nrDNA) and complete plastome
sequences, we examined the phylogenetic position of this species and explored the plastome variation
among the species of genus Bulbophyllum. The plastome of B. wolongense had a length of 157,496 bp
and encoded 132 genes. Comparative plastid genome analysis revealed that the plastome length of
B. wolongense was within the length range of other Bulbophyllum plastomes, and the plastome of
B. wolongense was relatively conserved in structure compared to other Bulbophyllum plastomes that
presented some genes (ndh, accD, and trnL-UAA) loss or pseudogenization, a small inversion, and inverted
repeat boundary expansions. Furthermore, phylogenetic analyses of 17 Bulbophyllum whole plastomes,
67 shared protein-coding genes from 17 Bulbophyllum whole plastomes, and 4 DNA (ITS, Xdh, matK, and
psbA-trnH) concatenated sequences from 127 Bulbophyllum species, all strongly supported that
B. wolongense is a member of the Cirrhopetalum alliance and most likely in B. sect. Brachyantha. Further
comparative morphological analysis showed that B. wolongense is clearly morphologically distinguishable
from its closely related species in the phylogenetic tree.
Introduction
Bulbophyllum Thouars (1822: t. 93; Malaxideae, Epidendroideae)
is one of the most species-rich genera of Orchidaceae, with 1,900
to 2,200 currently accepted species in the world, and with its
distribution extending from tropical Africa, Madagascar,
America, and mainland Asia to Australasia, which is an excellent
model system for elucidating the biodiversity of orchids [1–5].
Bulbophyllum species are characterized by a predominantly epi-
phytic or lithophytic growth form, creeping rhizome bearing a
series of 1- or 2-leaved pseudobulbs (very rarely absent), scape
arising laterally from the base of a pseudobulb or a rhizome
node, with flowers commonly featuring a fleshy and mobile
ligulate lip attached to a distinct column foot and morpholog-
ically diverse lateral sepals that vary in size, shape, color, and
surface ornamentation [4]. This large number of species and
high variation make the genus a considerable challenge for tax-
onomists: 120 sections and subgenera have been listed to date
[4,6]. Asia is the largest distribution region of Bulbophyllum with
67 recognized sections, and China is a biodiversity hotspot, with
>150 described species [4,7–10].
The “Cirrhopetalum alliance” is an association of sections
derived from Cirrhopetalum Lindl. (1830: 45), which was pre-
viously regarded as a genus separate from Bulbophyllum, but
most current orchid taxonomists accept its sectional status
within Bulbophyllum [2,4,11–13]. The members of this group
are usually characterized by subumbellate inflorescence, flowers
generally with fringed sepals and/or petals, lateral sepals much
longer than dorsal sepal, twisted at the base, and more or less
connate along their margins [4,12,14]. It is a species-rich group,

Wolong National Nature Reserve is located in the southwest
of Wenchuan County, Sichuan Province, which is the high
mountain valley area of transition from the Sichuan Basin to
Qinghai-Tibet Plateau. Its flora is part of the Hengduan Mountains
region in terms of the floristic geographical regionalism and is
among the global biodiversity hotspot regions [15,16]. During the
fieldwork at Wolong National Nature Reserve on 2021 October
11, a distinctive epiphytic orchid species was collected from 2
adjacent trunks of Taxus wallichiana var. chinensis (Pilg.) Florin,
at a cliffside of a valley, at an elevation of ca. 1,630 m above sea
level. Upon further investigations of other nearby T. wallichiana
var. chinensis as well as on other tree species, no additional popu-
lations were found. According to the preliminary morphological
characteristics, it was identified as an unusual Bulbophyllum spe-
cies belonging to the Cirrhopetalum alliance [4,14]. This species
is similar in size to B. reptans (sect. Reptantia) and B. omerandrum
(sect. Cirrhopetaloides), including its overall floral morphology,
but can easily be distinguished from the former by inflorescence
type number of inflorescences with flowers and also from the latter
by the shape of the dorsal sepals and petals, shape of the anther
cap, and flowering period. In addition, we compared its mor-
phology with that of B. chinense, a morphologically similar species
that has disappeared from China for many years, which is clearly
distinguished from the former by its rather large flowers, purple
petals and dorsal sepals, and green labellum (Table S3). After care-
fully comparing the plants with all previously known congeneric
taxa, it could not be matched with any known species. Hence, this
species was treated as a new species of the Cirrhopetalum alliance
(Bulbophyllum), closely related to the sections Brachyantha,
Cirrhopetaloides, Ephippium, and Macrostylidia, owing to shared
morphological character: basally twisted lateral sepals with con-
nate upper margins [2,4]. However, due to the ambiguous taxo-
nomic boundaries of these sections, it was difficult to determine
its sections from morphology alone. Therefore, it was necessary
to determine its phylogenetic position within Bulbophyllum
based on molecular evidence.
Here, we described and illustrated the new species Bulbophyllum
wolongense and assembled its nuclear ribosomal DNA (nrDNA)
and complete plastid genome sequences by the genome skimming
approach. The main aims of this study were to: (a) elucidate the
morphological characteristics of the new species, B. wolongense;
(b) determine the size range and structure of the B. wolongense
plastome; (c) characterize the variation of the 17 accessible
Bulbophyllum plastomes; and (d) explore the phylogenetic position
of B. wolongense within the genus Bulbophyllum. These results will
provide valuable and basic sequence information for Bulbophyllum
taxonomic studies and yield new insights into identification of
Bulbophyllum species.
Materials and Methods
Morphologic description and comparison
Morphological data of the new species were obtained during
fieldwork, and specimens were collected by Y. H. Cheng and
G. W. Hu and deposited at the Herbarium of Wuhan Botanical
Garden (HIB). Measurements, shapes, colors, and other
details given in the description were based on living materials.
Photographs of the flowering plants were taken with a Nikon
D850 digital camera. The floral anatomy was conducted under
an SMZ25 stereomicroscope.
Taxon sampling and DNA sequencing
Young fresh leaves of Bulbophyllum wolongense were sampled
from the Wolong National Nature Reserve. The sample collec-
tion was approved by the Sichuan Forestry Bureau and Sichuan
Wolong National Nature Reserve Administration, Sichuan
province, China. Total genomic DNA was extracted using a
modified cetyltrimethylammonium bromide method [17]. The
extracted DNA, after being purified, was used to construct
paired-end libraries by shearing the genomic DNA into short
fragments of approximately 350 bp and then sequenced in
150-bp paired-end mode on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 plat-
form (Illumina, Inc., San Diego, CA, USA) at Novogene Co.,
Ltd. (Beijing, China). High-quality reads of 6 GB were obtained.
The final number of raw reads obtained was 36,062,743, and
that of clean reads after filtering was 35,335,540.
Plastome and nrDNA assembly and annotation
The complete plastome and nrDNA (18S-ITS1–5.8S-ITS2-26S)
sequence of Bulbophyllum wolongense were assembled based on
clean reads obtained from genome skimming using GetOrganelle
v1.7.2a [18]. Final assembly graphs were visualized by Bandage
to assess the completeness of the final graph [19]. The resulting
complete plastid genome was automatically annotated using the
Plastid Genome Annotator [20], and all transfer RNA (tRNAs)
genes were proofread with tRNAscan-SE v2.0 [21]. The posi-
tions of the start codon, stop codon, and exon boundaries of all
genes were precisely verified and manually adjusted in Geneious
v8.0.2 [22]. The plastome map of B. wolongense was drawn
online using OGDRAW (http://ogdraw.mpimp-golm.mpg.de/)
[23]. The final whole plastome and nrDNA sequences of
B. wolongense were submitted to GenBank with accession num-
bers ON357877 and ON364003, respectively.
Genome features and comparative plastid
genomic analysis
Length of the whole plastome of Bulbophyllum wolongense,
number of genes, categories of genes, and guanine-cytosine
(GC) content were analyzed in Geneious v8.0.2 [22]. In order
to further identify potential variations between B. wolongense
and other 16 Bulbophyllum species, the genome rearrangement
analyses of these Bulbophyllum sequences were performed using
the progressiveMauve algorithm [24] plugined in Geneious v8.0.2
[22]. Inverted repeat (IR) expansion/contraction of these plas-
tomes was also checked using IRscope [25], and the graph of
expansions/contractions was beautified using Adobe Illustrator.
Additionally, gene loss in complete plastid genomes of the 17
Bulbophyllum species was visualized utilizing HeatMap tool in
TBtools software [26].

Phylogenetic analyses
To speculate on the phylogenetic position of the new Bulbophyllum
species, phylogenies were reconstructed by maximum likelihood
(ML) and Bayesian inference (BI) methods using the 3 datasets:
(a) complete plastome sequences; (b) shared protein-coding gene
(PCG) sequences; (c) concatenated sequences of 2 nuclear DNA
(ITS and Xdh) and 2 plastid DNA (matK and psbA-trnH). For
the whole plastid genome dataset, all the published plastomes of
17 Bulbophyllum were used to construct phylogenetic trees. For
the nuclear gene and plastid gene concatenated dataset, a total of
127 species of Bulbophyllum, representing all putatively associ-
ated sections with the Cirrhopetalum alliance [14] and the major-
ity of species in the plastome dataset, were included in the
phylogenetic analyses. In all phylogenetic trees, Dendrobium

2 nrDNA and 2 plastid DNA alignments were concatenated
into 1 supermatrix by PhyloSuite v1.2.2 [30]. Substitutional
saturation was assessed using DAMBE v 7.0.68 [31] for the
concatenated matrix. ModelFinder [32] was utilized to select
the best-fit model for accurate phylogenetic estimates, the best-
fit models chosen for ML and BI phylogenetic analyses of dif-
ferent datasets were presented in Table S6. ML analysis was
conducted using IQ-TREE v1.6.8 [33] with 1,000 bootstrap
replicates, whereas BI analysis was performed in MrBayes
v3.2.6 [34]. For BI analysis, the Markov chain Monte Carlo
algorithm was run for 2 × 2,000,000 generations. Phylogenetic
trees were sampled every 1,000 generations with the first 25%
of the trees were discarded as burn-in, and the remaining
trees were used to construct majority-rule consensus tree [34].
Finally, the generated phylogenetic trees were visualized using
Figtree v1.4.4 (http://tree.bio.ed.ac.uk/software/figtree/) and
further embellished by Adobe Illustrator software.

Phenology: Flowering was observed in the field from September
to October (the full-bloom stage in early October).
Etymology: The specific epithet “wolongense” refers to the
locality of the type specimen, Wolong National Nature Reserve,
Wenchuan county, Sichuan, China.
Distribution and habitat: Bulbophyllum wolongense has so
far been collected only in Wolong National Nature Reserve,
Sichuan, China. It is epiphytic on the trunk of Taxus wallichiana
var. chinensis at a cliff edge of a valley, in a habitat with canopy
density of ca. 60% to 70%, at an elevation of ca. 1,630 m.
Conservation status: Bulbophyllum wolongense is known
from only the type locality, and only one population of ca.
150 individuals was discovered in a small area. However, more
field studies are needed for assessment of its true conservation
status. According to the IUCN criteria [35], it may be tenta-
tively assessed at present as “Data Deficient”.

Characteristics of the Bulbophyllum
wolongense plastome
The complete plastome of Bulbophyllum wolongense was 157,496 bp
in length and exhibited a typical quadripartite structure, consisting
of 1 large single copy (LSC) region of 85,983 bp and 1 small single
copy (SSC) region of 17,819 bp, which were separated by a pair of
26,847 bp IR regions (IRA and IRB) (Fig. 4 and Table). The total
GC content of the whole plastome was 36.97%, and the GC content
of IR region (43.05%) was higher than that of the LSC region
(34.62%) and SSC region (30.00%). A total of 132 genes were anno-
tated in the plastome of B. wolongense, including 86 PCGs, 38
tRNA genes, and 8 ribosomal RNA (rRNA) genes (Table). These
genes could be divided into 4 categories: genes for genetic system,
genes for photosynthesis system, genes for biosynthesis, and PCGs
with unknown functions (Table S4). Most of genes appeared as a
single copy in LSC or SSC, whereas 19 genes were duplicated in
the IR regions, of which 7 were PCGs (ndhB, rpl2, rpl23, rps7,
rps12, rps19, and ycf2), 8 were tRNA genes (trnA-UGC, trnH-GUG,
trnI-CAU, trnI-GAU, trnL-CAA, trnN-GUU, trnR-ACG, and trnV-
GAC), and 4 were rRNA genes (rrn4.5, rrn5, rrn16, and rrn23)
(Fig. 4 and Table S4). In addition, a total of 18 genes possessed
introns: 15 genes (9 PCGs: rps16, atpF, rpoC1, petB, petD, rpl16,
rpl2, ndhB, and ndhA; 6 tRNA genes: trnK-UUU, trnG-UCC, trnL-
UAA, trnV-UAC, trnI-GAU, and trnA-UGC) contained a single

Comparative analysis of the basic characteristics of plastid geno-
mes of Bulbophyllum wolongense and other 16 Bulbophyllum spe-
cies showed that the full plastome lengths of the 17 Bulbophyllum
species ranged from 146,401 bp (B. plumosum) to 158,524 bp
(B. gedangense) (Table). Moreover, the range of length variation
between the LSC and SSC regions of the Bulbophyllum plastomes
was obvious, with lengths of 79,001 to 87,194 and 11,089 to 18,632,
respectively. The 17 Bulbophyllum plastomes were all AT-rich, and
their overall GC content varied from 36.56% (B. plumosum) to
37.94% (B. disciflorum). Interestingly, B. disciflorum possessed the
highest GC content in the LSC, SSC, and IR regions (35.80%,
30.93%, and 43.37%, respectively), B. plumosum had the lowest

Phylogenetic analysis
Although the topologies based on complete plastome and
shared PCG datasets are similar, the phylogenetic results of
plastid genome sequences were chosen for discussion due to
their better resolution and branching node support (Fig. 7 and
Fig. S2). Phylogenetic result of complete plastome dataset
strongly supported that Bulbophyllum wolongense was sister to
the highlighted clade comprising B. gedangense (sect. Desmo-
santhes) + B. pingnanense (sect. Cirrhopeta lum) + B. inconspicuum
(sect. Cirrhopetalum) (Fig. 7, BP, posterior probability [PP] =
100%, 1.00, respectively). Topologies of phylogenetic trees
obtained from combined plastid DNA and nrDNA dataset
using ML and BI methods were slightly different, but both
indicated that B. wolongense was nested within the clade
comprised of B. andersonii (sect. Ephippium) + B. tianguii
(sect. Brachyantha) + B. umbellatum (sect. Brachyantha) +
B. fuscopurpureum (sect. Cirrhopetaloides) + B. picturatum
(sect. Cirrhopetaloides) + B. schwarzii (sect. Cirrhopetalum) +
B. amplifolium (sect. Cirrhopetalum) + B. violaceolabellum
(sect. Brachyantha) + B. guttulatum (sect. Brachyantha) +
B. fenestratum (sect. Cirrhopetalum) + B. fimbriatum (sect. Cirrho-
petalum) + B. delitescens (sect. Macrostylida) + B. macraei
(sect. Macrostylida) + B. longiflorum (sect. Cirrhopetalum) +
B. weberi (sect. Cirrhopetalum) + B. longibrachiatum (sect.
Cirrhopetalum) + B. sarcophylloides (sect. Brachyantha) with
a strong support (Fig. 8 and Fig. S3; BP, PP = 100%, 1.00,
respectively). In addition, in all sampled species, all Cirrhopetalum
alliance species formed a monophyletic clade with a strong
support (Fig. 8 and Fig. S3; BP, PP = 100%, 1.00, respectively),
but the relationships among most of the sections were not well
resolved.
Discussion
The complete plastome of Bulbophyllum wolongense was sequenced,
assembled, annotated, and analyzed in comparison with all avail-
able Bulbophyllum plastomes obtained from NCBI. As reported for
most angiosperms [36–38], the plastome of B. wolongense displayed
a typical quadripartite structure. In terms of genome size, B. wolon-
gense plastome (157,496 bp) fell within the size range of
Bulbophyllum species [39–41]. Like in other Bulbophyllum plas-
tomes, the GC content of IR region (43.05%) in the B. wolongense
plastome was higher than that of LSC and SSC regions (34.62%
and 30.00%, respectively) (Table). This high GC percentage in the
IR regions could be related to the presence of all rRNA genes in
this region, as supported by previous studies [42]. A total of 132
genes were identified in the plastome, with 86 PCGs, 38 tRNAs,
and all 8 rRNAs (Table and Table S4). There were no gene losses or
pseudogenes (except for rpl22 and ycf1 across the IR border) in the
B. wolongense plastome. However, comparison of the plastid
genomes revealed that photosynthesis-related ndh genes were

clade were consistent with a recent work on Bulbophyllum
phylogeny, which also indicated that it is difficult to identify
synapomorphies due to the heterogeneity of floral characters
among constituent members of this clade [14]. From the pres-
ent phylogeny, it appears that B. wolongense is relatively closely
related to species in the B. sect. Brachyantha (B. violaceola-
bellum and B. guttulatum), but the clade support values are
remarkably low. Additionally, some morphological characters
(1-leaved pseudobulbs; inflorescence with 1 to 3 flowers arranged
in sub-umbellate raceme; lateral sepals that are ca. 2 times
longer than the dorsal one, twisted near their base so that the
upper margins usually turn inwards and are free along their
upper margins; petals entire with erose margins; labellum ca.
4.5 mm; stelidia tapering toward an acute apex) inclined toward
the possibility that B. wolongense belongs to the B. sect. Brachyantha
[14]. However, the taxonomic delimitations of the 5 sec-
tions (B. sects. Cirrhopetalum, Brachyantha, Cirrhopetaloides,
Ephippium, and Macrostylidia) are blurred, and most sections
within the Cirrhopetalum alliance have been demonstrated to be
polyphyletic or paraphyletic, necessitating a new sectional classi-
fication. Despite this, our treatment of B. wolongense as a new
species is supported by its independent position as a sister clade
to other Bulbophyllum species and the morphological differences
from B. omerandrum, B. reptans, B. chinense, B. violaceolabellum,
and B. guttulatum (Table S3). Nevertheless, more data is necessary
to resolve the taxonomic complexities in the larger clade where
B. wolongense is nested.
