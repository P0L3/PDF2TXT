1 Introduction
The overarching goal of ERA4CS-MEDSCOPE project
(www. medsc ope- proje ct. eu) co-funded by JPI-Climate and
the European Union Horizon 2020 program from 2017 to
2021 was to enhance the uptake of climate predictions at
the seasonal-to-decadal timescale over the Mediterranean
region. One facet of its activities was the improvement of
the understanding of mechanisms driving climate variabil-
ity and predictability over the Mediterranean basin at these
timescales.
The thirteen papers included in this Special Issue are
from authors involved in the project or in similar research
areas and tackle several key topics: from understanding the
sources of climate variability over the Mediterranean region
and scale interactions, to analyzing the current capabilities
of state-of-the-art operational weather and climate predic-
tion systems. This corpus of work provides a valuable over-
view of current knowledge for readers interested in Mediter-
ranean climate variability and predictability.
2 Understanding sources of variability
over the Mediterranean region
In the framework of MEDSCOPE, several sets of idealized
sensitivity experiments were designed to investigate sources
of predictability over the Euro-Atlantic and Mediterranean
regions.

Building on past works evidencing the influence of soil
moisture feedbacks on Mediterranean climate at the seasonal
timescale (e.g. Lorenz et al. 2010; Hirschi et al. 2011), a
first set of idealized experiments investigated the role of
extreme soil moisture anomalies over the region with two
atmosphere-land surface models. The first two papers of the
Special Issue thus report on the influence of extreme soil
moisture conditions on precipitation (Ardilouze et al. 2020)
and temperature in a subset of regions in a transitional cli-
mate regime (Materia et al. 2021), in both prescribed and
coupled land–atmosphere simulations. Their findings high-
light the importance of modeling land–atmosphere coupling
processes for capturing large-scale persistent events at sub-
seasonal to seasonal time scales.
Another source of variability at the seasonal time scale
investigated during the project is El Niño-Southern Oscil-
lation (ENSO). The research implemented in MEDSCOPE
builds on a large corpus of work dedicated to ENSO influ-
ence on seasonal variability and predictability (e.g. Brönni-
mann 2007). Idealized experiments with three atmospheric
global circulation models were set up so as to investigate
the (a)symmetry of the atmospheric response to canonical
positive and negative ENSO SST patterns. The setup is fully
described in Mezzina et al. (2020), which disentangles the
origin of asymmetries in the late-winter sea-level pressure
response over the Northern Hemisphere to positive and neg-
ative ENSO patterns. In the following paper of the Special
Issue, the asymmetry of the response to ENSO is further
analyzed in these experiments, focusing on the signal in the
stratosphere (Mezzina et al. 2021). Results suggest the sea-
level pressure response to ENSO is mainly driven by tropo-
spheric processes.
Additional idealized experiments were implemented in
the project to investigate the modulation of the influence
of ENSO on the Northern Hemisphere atmosphere dynam-
ics according to phases of the Pacific Decadal Oscillation
(PDO). Benassi et al. (2021) evidence a robust modulation

Ruggieri et al. (2022) provide insight on another poten-

Three contributions focus on other time scales. Hochman
et al. (2020) use a dynamical system approach and numerical
weather predictions to understand synoptic-scale predict-
ability of extreme cold events in the Eastern Mediterranean.
Liu et al. (2021) study the decadal variability of the East-
ern Mediterranean upper-layer circulation with a 110-year
regional ocean model simulation. Using canonical correla-
tion analyses of twentieth century reanalysis and observa-
tion datasets, Suárez-Moreno et al. (2021) separate natural
variability and anthropogenic forcing effects on precipitation
and temperature, focusing on the Mediterranean wet season.
3 Current state‑of‑the‑art prediction skill
After identifying and disentangling possible sources of pre-
dictability for the Mediterranean region, an essential step
towards the use and uptake of climate predictions is the
evaluation of the skill in current state-of-the-art forecast-
ing systems. Since re-forecast periods are limited and skill
levels over the region lower than over the Tropics, studies
from the Special Issue have striven to assess the representa-
tion of mechanisms of predictability in seasonal prediction
systems, and provide robust analyses of skill using statistical
benchmarks.
Portal et al. (2021) focus on the seasonal prediction of
the stratosphere during boreal winter in state-of-the-art C3S
systems, evidencing strengths and weaknesses of models in
representing key stratospheric processes such as sudden
stratospheric warmings and lower stratosphere wave activ-
ity. The study by Ruggieri et al. (2022) identifies that C3S
seasonal re-forecasts exhibit surface energy balance and cir-
culation responses to snow cover anomalies consistent with
idealized simulations.
Synoptic-scale circulation over the Mediterranean in
boreal winter can be classified into four weather regimes.
Giuntoli et al. (2021) describe these weather regimes and
evaluate how they are represented and predicted by C3S sea-
sonal prediction systems. Prodhomme et al. (2021) defined
heatwave indices and evaluated their predictability over
Europe in the ECMWF seasonal prediction System 5. They
find the model outperforms a simple statistical model over
the Mediterranean and Eastern Europe regions. An over-
all assessment of seasonal prediction skill in several C3S
systems and a multi-model ensemble for May and November
start dates is provided in Calì Quaglia et al. (2021). Dynami-
cal systems outperform statistical forecasts for several key
aspects of forecast quality for temperature and precipitation
over the Mediterranean region.
4 Perspectives
The ERA4CS-MEDSCOPE project and related initiatives
have contributed to a broader understanding of sources of
variability and predictability of weather and climate from
synoptic to decadal time scales, as demonstrated by the
selection of publications included in this Special Issue.
This understanding is a first and mandatory stepping
stone towards the use of seasonal forecasts for operational
decision-making (Kumar et al. 2020). Use cases for climate
forecasts over the Mediterranean were demonstrated during
MEDSCOPE, and skilful forecasts depend on the ability of
dynamical, empirical and hybrid systems to represent cli-
mate drivers, climate variability patterns and teleconnections
that are relevant at these time scales.
Shortcomings in current state-of-the-art systems have
been evidenced in several contributions to this Special Issue,
which will guide future developments to enhance predictive
skill over the region.
