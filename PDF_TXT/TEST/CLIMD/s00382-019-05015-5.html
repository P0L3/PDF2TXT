Abstract
A major sub-seasonal variability of the tropics and sub-tropics, the quasi-biweekly mode (QBM), is known to have significant
influence on the seasonal mean of the south Asian monsoon rainfall. A coupled Atmosphere–Ocean General Circulation
Model (AOGCM) being essential for seasonal prediction, the ability of the AOGCMs in simulating the space–time char-
acteristics with fidelity is critical for successful seasonal prediction of the south Asian monsoon in particular and seasonal
prediction in the tropics in general. However, strength and weaknesses in simulating the QBM by AOGCMs have remained
poorly investigated so far. Here, we examine the simulation of the QBM in AOGCM and show that improvement of param-
eterizations of both convection and microphysics is required to improve the simulation of the QBM. While the standard ver-
sion of the model overestimates the variance of QBM and simulates a smaller scale Rossby waves (n = 1), the modified version
of the model where the simple Arakawa–Schubert (SAS) convection parameterization is combined with a new improved
microphysics parameterization (MCMv.1) proposed by us, simulates a more realistic space–time characteristics of the
QBM. In yet another version of the model, we combine the new SAS with the new improved microphysics parameterization.
Interestingly, this version of the model also simulates the space–time structure poorly with poor westward propagation and
fragmented organization, but it simulates a reasonable variance. These results indicate that a synergy among the convective
parameterization and microphysics parameterizations is critical in simulating the QBM in particular and equatorial waves
in general. We show that most the biases in simulating the QBM may be related to the biases of the model in simulating the
stratiform fraction of precipitation. While the simulation of the space–time characteristics of QBM is better simulated in the
MCMv.1, the convective coupling is still too strong as compared to observations, an area for future improvement of the model.
Keywords Indian summer monsoon · Quasi-biweekly mode (QBM) · CFSv2 · Modified convective microphysics

mean climate of the tropics and the sub-tropics. Studied

Atmosphere–Ocean General Circulation Model (AOGCM)
is critical for the simulation and prediction of the Indian
summer monsoon (Webster et al. 1998; Wang et al. 2005).
Also it is found that there is considerable air-sea interaction
associated with the QBM (Sengupta et al. 2004; Ye and Wu
2015; Hu and Li 2017). Reinforcing earlier arguments, these
findings make an AOGCM an essential tool for seasonal
forecasting of the ISMR. However, seasonal forecast models
used commonly for operational seasonal forecasting are of
relatively low atmospheric resolution. Do these AOGCMs
simulate the QBM with high fidelity? What is required to
improve the simulation of the QBM in such models? In this
study, we examine the simulation of the QBM in such an
AOGCM and show that improvement of parameterizations
of both convection and microphysics is required to improve
the simulation of the QBM.
The dominant temporal characteristics, namely the quasi-
biweekly period between 10 and 20 days with a preference
of about 15-days as well as its dominant westward propagat-
ing character of the QBM was noted in several early studies
(Murakami 1975; Krishnamurti and Bhalme 1976; Krishna-
murti and Ardanuy 1980). With the availability of better
three dimensional atmospheric observation, the typical
horizontal and vertical structures associated with the QBM
over the Indian monsoon region were brought out (Chen and
Chen 1993; Chatterjee and Goswami 2004), which reveal
that the horizontal spatial structure of the mode bears close
resemblance to a n = 1 equatorial Rossby wave with west-
ward phase speed of approximately 4.5 ms−1. However, it
was found that the pair of vortices associated with the mode
is centered around 5° N. This is due to the fact that in the
presence of a mean flow, the two vortices of the n = 1 equa-
torial Rossby wave would be centered around the dynamic
equator rather than the geographic equator (Chatterjee and
Goswami 2004). They have also shown that very similar
QBM occurs in the southern tropics during Austral summer
indicating the universal nature of the QBM. Strong convec-
tive coupling is evident for the mode with almost a weakly
baroclinic vertical structure (Chen and Chen 1993; Chatter-
jee and Goswami 2004; Chen and Sui 2010). The spatial and
temporal scale selection for the QBM is supported by theo-
retical analysis of an n = 1 equatorial Rossby wave driven
unstable by convective coupling (Chatterjee and Goswami
2004). While exploring the life cycle and global perspective
of the QBM, Kikuchi and Wang (2009) have shown that the
QBM also has an eastward propagating component in the
sub-tropics, whose initiation and propagation are controlled
by extra-tropical barotropic Rossby wave trains.
The QBM may be considered as the high frequency com-
ponent of the monsoon intra-seasonal oscillations (MISOs)
with the northward propagating 30–60 day mode representing
the lower frequency spectrum of MISOs and explaining about
half of the sub-seasonal variance (see Goswami 2012, for a
review). While the simulations of the 30–60 day mode by vari-
ous models have been studied extensively (e.g., Sabeerali et al.
2013), simulation of QBM by models has received very little
attention so far. In this study, we examine the simulation of
QBM by one coupled AOGCM, namely the Climate Forecast
System version 2 (CFSv2) of National Centers for Environ-
mental Prediction (NCEP; Saha et al. 2010, 2014a, b). Adopt-
ing this model as the base model under the National Monsoon
Mission of India, a seasonal forecast system has been set up
for operational seasonal forecasting of the monsoon and some
notable developments in improving the model has been made
(Hazra et al. 2015, 2016, 2017a, b; Bombardi et al. 2016; Saha
et al. 2017; Goswami et al. 2017; Abhik et al. 2017; Pokhrel
et al. 2018). A well documented (Dai 2006; Yin et al. 2013;
Ahmed and Schumacher 2015; Sabeerali et al. 2015; Chaud-
hari et al. 2018; Pathak et al. 2019) systematic bias in most of
the coupled climate models is that they tend to simulate too
much convective rain and too little stratiform rain as compared
to observations. The implication is that the simulated verti-
cal structure of diabatic heating has projection largely on the
first baroclinic mode, while the observed vertical structure has
significant projection on second baroclinic mode as well. The
observed propensity of the stratiform rain fraction is known
to play a significant role in organization and propagation of
the 30–60 day mode (Kumar et al. 2017). As the QBM is a
strongly convectively coupled equatorial wave, it is pertinent
that the stratiform rain fraction and resulting vertical structure
of heating are equally important for the space–time structure of
the QBM. It has been seen that improvement of cloud micro-
physics in a coupled model leads to improvement of simula-
tion of stratiform rain fraction and significant improvement in
simulation of the 30–60 day mode (Hazra et al. 2017b). These
studies provide a scientific basis to think that simulation of
QBM in a coupled model may also benefit from improved
microphysics parameterization. With this background, we
learn some lessons in this study, fronting the biases the simu-
lation of the QBM in the control version of the model and how
improvement of microphysics parameterizations in the model
lead to improvement of simulations of the observed characters
of the QBM.
This study is organized as follows: Sect.  2 describes
details about data and model experiments. Section 3 elab-
orates the results of the study. Section 4 gives the details
about discussion and conclusions of the study.
2 Data and model experiments
The Climate Forecast System version 2 (CFSv2; Saha
et al. 2010, 2014a, b) of NCEP, used here is a fully cou-
pled ocean‐atmosphere‐land model with advanced physics,
to improve the seasonal climate forecasts. It has a spectral

64 hybrid vertical levels while the Modular Ocean Model
version 4p0d (Griffies et al. 2004) at 0.25–0.5° grid spacing
with 40 vertical layers from Geophysical Fluid Dynamics
Laboratory (GFDL) is used for ocean model. The resolu-
tion of ocean model (Modular Ocean Model version 4p0d;
Griffies et al. 2004) varies from 0.25° to 0.5°. At Equator
it has resolution of 0.25 and it becomes coarse (i.e., 0.5) as
we go towards poles. The atmosphere and ocean models
are coupled with no flux adjustment. It utilizes simplified
Arakawa–Schubert (SAS) cumulus convection (Hong and
Pan 1998) with momentum mixing and the new SAS (nSAS)
(Han and Pan 2011) convective parameterization scheme,
which is believed to make cumulus convection stronger and
deeper, and to deplete more instability in the atmospheric
column (Han and Pan 2011) in the atmospheric component
of the CFS model. It implements orographic gravity wave
drag based on Kim and Arakawa (1995) approach and sub
grid Scale Mountain blocking by Lott and Miller (1997).
It uses rapid radiative transfer model (RRTM) shortwave
radiation with maximum random cloud overlap (Iacono et al.
2000; Clough et al. 2005). It is also coupled to a four-layer
Noah land surface model (Ek et al. 2003) and a two-layer sea
ice model (Wu et al. 2005). In CFSv2, the control version
of cloud microphysics parameterization scheme is of Zhao
and Carr (1997), hereafter referred to as ZC97, which in turn
is based on Sundqvist et al. (1989). The predictable vari-
ables namely, cloud mixing ratio included to the prognostic
equations of ZC97 cloud scheme for the representation of
cloud ice and cloud water. The thermodynamic effect of con-
densation is important due to the linkage between radiative,
dynamical and hydrological processes. ZC97 cloud scheme
in CFSv2 considers two supplies of condensations (namely,
convective condensation and large-scale condensation). The
convective condensation, following Betts and Miller (1986)
is used in CFSv2 to account for the inclusion of the prog-
nostic variables (e.g., cloud ice and cloud water, ZC97). On
the other hand, the expression for the large-scale conden-
sation is obtained as mentioned in Sundqvist (1988) and
Zhao and Carr (1997). The microphysical processes (e.g.,
snow autoconversion, collection of cloud ice by snow and
snow melting etc.) for precipitation calculation in CFSv2
model follows ZC97 whereas, the precipitation calculation
is carried out by the autoconversion of cloud water to rain,
following Sundqvist et al. (1989). In modified versions of
the NCEP CFSv2 model, a physically based modified con-
vective microphysics scheme is implemented. Further, a
major modification has been carried out to the existing cloud
microphysics scheme in the CFSv2 (Hazra et al. 2017b) with
the guidance of some recent observations under the Cloud
Aerosol Interaction and Precipitation Enhancement Experi-
ment (CAIPEEX) of IITM. The modifications of partitioning
of cloud water and ice in the existing SAS and nSAS (new
601
SAS) convective schemes have also been carried out (Hazra
et al. 2017b). The details of the cloud scheme (control and
modification) is available in Hazra et al. (2017a, b). In this
study the following three versions of model are employed:
(1) the standard version of NCEP CFSv2 (Control, CTL),
(2) modified cloud microphysics together with modified
SAS convection scheme (MCMv.1) and (3) modified cloud
microphysics together with modified new SAS convection
scheme (MCMv.2). A 20-year long coupled free run for each
version of model is carried out. The details of the schemes
(i.e., MCMv.1 and MCMv.2) have been documented in
Hazra et al. (2017b). Last 15-years of each run have been
used for the analysis presented here and 5 years are left out
for spin-up.
The observed QBM is calculated from two different pre-
cipitation data sets, namely the Global Precipitation Clima-
tology Project (GPCP; Adler et al. 2003) and Tropical Rain
Measuring Mission (TRMM 3B42v7; Huffman et al. 2007).
To bring out the QBM, a 10–20 day filter on daily anomalies
using a Lanczos filter (Duchon 1979) is used on each field.
3 Results
It has been already reported (Hazra et al. 2017b) that the
mean state of Indian summer monsoon (ISM) rainfall and
the dry biases of the model have been improved significantly
by the modified convective microphysics scheme as com-
pared to the standard version of CFSv2 (control). The biases
in the mean ISM may be due to biases in the simulation of
the low-frequency MISO. Therefore, in our earlier attempt
we documented the improvement of low-frequency MISO
simulation in the CFSv2 with modified convective micro-
physical scheme and how it leads to improvement of the
seasonal mean Indian monsoon. In the present endeavour,
we shall address the following important question: how the
modified convective microphysics (MCM) parameteriza-
tion improves the simulation of the variance and space–time
spectra of the high-frequency MISO (10–20 days time scale,
namely the QBM)?

overestimates QBM variance almost everywhere (Fig. 1b).

3.2 The QBM simulated by improved
parameterization of convection
and microphysics
As mentioned before, in MCMv.1 version of the model,
the new formulation of cloud microphysics (Hazra et al.
2017b) has been incorporated in conjunction with the SAS
convective parameterization, while in the MCMv.2 the same
is incorporated in conjunction with the new SAS (nSAS)
convective parameterization. The improved microphysics
parameterization has been shown to improve the simula-
tion of stratiform rain fraction leading to improvement in
simulation of the 30–60 day mode together with signifi-
cant improvement in dry bias over the Indian sub-continent
(Hazra et al. 2017b). As for the simulation of QBM, it may
be noted that the MCMv.1 still overestimates the QBM
variance (10–20 day) over oceanic regions, with a large
extension over the Bay of Bengal (BoB) and the Arabian
Sea (Fig. 3a), which is reduced significantly in MCMv.2
(Fig.  3b). However, MCMv.1 simulates QBM variance
more realistically over central India and other land parts of
India (e.g., northeast region) as compared to MCMv.2. The
QBM variance is reasonably well simulated over the oceanic
regions (Fig. 1) in MCMv.2, while significant underestima-
tion exists over the north BoB and northeast India and north-
west India. Over the land region however MCMv.1 is better
for high frequency MISO variance (QBM).

The structure of the winds and precipitation associated with
phase 8 (trough) and phase 1 (peak) of the mode in obser-
vations and model simulations are shown in Fig. 6. While
all versions of the model simulates an equatorial Rossby

MCMv.1 coming closer to the observed structure. These
observations are consistent with the space–time spectra dis-

4 Conclusions and discussions
The simulation of the space–time characteristics of the QBM
by AOGCMs with fidelity is a critical requirement for suc-
cess of seasonal prediction of south Asian monsoon rainfall
in particular and seasonal prediction in tropics in general,
which has received little attention so far. In this endeavor,
we learn some lessons by noting biases in the simulation
of the QBM in the control version of an AOGCM and how
improvement of convection and microphysics parameteriza-
tions in the model lead to improvement of simulations of the
observed characters of the QBM.
For this purpose, we use Climate Forecast system, ver-
sion 2 (CFSv2) of National Centre Environmental Predic-
tion (NCEP) and examine simulation of the QBM in three
different versions of the model. The standard version of the
model overestimates the variance of QBM and simulates
a smaller scale Rossby waves (n = 1). In the modified ver-
sion of the model, where the simple Arakawa–Schubert
(SAS) convection parameterization is combined with a new
improved microphysics parameterization proposed by us,
simulation of the space–time characteristics are is much

We show that most of the biases in simulating the QBM
may be related to the biases of the model in simulating the
stratiform fraction of precipitation. The propagation charac-
teristics and spatial organization of the low frequency MISO
(30–60 day mode) is known to be closely linked with the ver-
tical structure of the heating controlled largely by contribu-
tion of stratiform fraction of precipitation (Schumacher et al.
2004; Chattopadhyay et al. 2009; Kumar et al. 2017). Our
results indicate that the same is true for the QBM as well.
The generic problem with almost all coupled models is that
they highly overestimate contribution of convective precipi-
tation thereby underestimating the stratiform fraction (e.g.,
Sabeerali et al. 2015). The CFSv.2 model is no exception to
this problem (Fig. 7b) with massive overestimation of convec-
tive precipitation over warm tropical oceans and underesti-
mation of it over cold tropical oceans. The new microphysics
parameterization in conjunction with SAS convection scheme
(MCMv.1) leads to decrease in convective fraction almost eve-
rywhere but most markedly over land regions of south and
south-east Asia and over Amazon. While the convective frac-
tion still remains higher than observed over oceans, it comes

a synergy between the convection and microphysics param-

and equatorial waves in general. Our finding that when we

zation is critical for the simulation of the stratiform fraction,
its connectivity with the background convection parameteri-
zation is equally important. The source of moisture content
and triggering of convection through convection scheme are
important component for the microphysics scheme to form
proper cloud condensates (i.e., cloud water and cloud ice).
While significant improvement in simulating the
space–time characteristics of the QBM has been achieved in
the MCMv.1, we still have not found a model for simulating
the QBM ‘perfectly’. However, our experiments and analysis
have provided some directions for further improvements.
The phase propagation of the convectively coupled equato-
rial waves is dependent on the strength of the convective
coupling. Much slower than observed simulated westward
propagation characteristics even in the case of MCMv.1
(Fig. 4c) is indicative of a stronger convective coupling in
the model. This is consistent with the simulation of overall
larger than observed convective fraction of precipitation by
the model even though it is closer to observations over land.
In addition to the required reduction of simulated convective
fraction of precipitation, the convective coupling may also
depend on how the boundary layer model feeds heat and
moisture fluxes to the convective elements. Therefore, an
optimum synergy (in terms of proper feedback among vari-
ous physical processes) would be required among the bound-
ary layer model (i.e. land surface processes), the convection
parameterization and the microphysics parameterization.
