Abstract Heat content anomalies are analyzed to
understand subsurface variability on both interannual
and decadal time scales in the Paci"c Ocean, with a
particular focus on the evolving basinwide patterns and
oceanic connections between the extratropics and
tropics. Various analyses indicate two distinct modes,
one interannual and the other decadal, that involve the
tropics and the North Paci"c subtropical gyre, respec-
tively. Interannual variability is associated with El
Nin8 o in the tropics, with a prominent &&see-saw’’ pattern
alternately on and o! the equator, and in the east and
west, respectively. The interannual cycle features a co-
herent propagation of subsurface signals around the
tropical Paci"c, eastward along the equator but west-
ward o! the equator at 10}15 3N. Decadal signals are
dominant in the subtropics and midlatitudes but also
have a tropical component that appears to be indepen-
dent of interannual variations. An oceanic connection
can be seen between subsurface anomalies in the mid-
latitudes, in the subtropics and tropics on decadal time
scales. Subsurface thermal anomalies associated with
midlatitude decadal variability can propagate through
the subtropics into the tropics, which may modulate
the intensity of interannual variability in the tropics.
For example, in the middle and late 1970s, a signi"cant
warm temperature anomaly appeared to penetrate into
the western and central tropics at depth, warming the
tropical upper ocean and depressing the thermocline.

extratropically preconditioned subsurface state (e.g., an
enhanced positive heat content anomaly) in the western

1 Introduction
The scienti"c community is making great progress to-
wards understanding interannual and decadal climate
variability in the Paci"c Ocean (e.g., Philander 1992;
Levitus and Antonov 1995; Latif et al. 1997). In the
tropics, low-frequency variations are dominated by in-
terannual variability associated with El Nin8 o, which
has been successfully investigated through observa-
tions and models (e.g., Busalacchi and O’Brien 1981;
McCreary and Anderson 1984; Cane and Zebiak 1985;
Philander 1990; Jin and Neelin 1993; and many others).
The El Nin8 o cycle is the dominant interannual mode in
the tropical coupled ocean-atmosphere system (Schopf
and Suarez 1988; Graham and White 1988; Zebiak
1989; Battisti and Hirst 1989). Various studies further
suggest that ocean processes play an important role in
self-sustaining this continual oscillation on interannual
time scales, manifested as a coherent phase propaga-
tion of subsurface anomalies around the tropics, which,
in due course, in#uences SST changes in the eastern
equatorial Paci"c (Chao and Philander 1993; Zhang
and Levitus 1996). Decadal variability in the tropics is
clearly evident as well. Recent observations indicate

explain decadal variations in the North Paci"c as an
atmospheric teleconnection to the tropics (Bjerknes

rotate clockwise around the North Paci"c subtropical
gyre. This dynamical "nding has been con"rmed from
three-dimensional ocean temperature observations
(Zhang and Levitus 1997). Nevertheless, the cause of
midlatitude ocean variability and its role in the Paci"c
decadal oscillation (PDO) are still in debate.
There is growing evidence that the midlatitudes
and tropics can be connected within the ocean itself
on interannual and decadal time scales. It has been
demonstrated that tropical interannual variability as-
sociated with the 1982}83 El Nin8 o may have had a dec-

the midlatitudes (Jacobs et al. 1994). Recent modeling
studies and data analyses suggest that the oceano-
graphic component of midlatitude decadal variability
has remote e!ects on the tropics as well (e.g., Liu and
Philander 1995; Gu and Philander 1997; Lysne et al.
1997; Zhang et al. 1998). It has been further demon-
strated that the decadal component of low-frequency
ocean variability over the Paci"c could be independent
of interannual variations that originate primarily from
the tropics associated with El Nin8 o (e.g., Kawamura
1994; Deser and Blackmon 1995; Yukimoto et al. 1996;
Zhang et al. 1997; Latif et al. 1997; Knutson and
Manabe 1998). Clearly, the relationships between in-
terannual and decadal variability in the tropics, sub-
tropics and in the midlatitudes have yet to be estab-
lished; the detailed spatial structure of the subsurface
connections between the extratropics and tropics needs
to be examined further on the basis of observational
data.
As demonstrated by numerous studies, interannual
and decadal variability can be more coherently de-
scribed and better understood by studying basin-
wide subsurface thermal anomalies. Based on upper
ocean temperature observations (Levitus et al. 1994),
this work will examine basinwide subsurface varia-
bility in the Paci"c basin on interannual and decadal
time scales, with emphasis on the evolving patterns

2 Observational data
The data we used were the yearly in situ temperature anomaly "elds
provided by Levitus et al. (1994). These "elds represent objective
analyses of historical temperature pro"les available at the National
Oceanographic Data Center (NODC/NOAA) through December
1993. These observational pro"les have been analyzed in a consis-
tent, objective manner on a 13 latitude-longitude grid at standard
oceanographic analysis levels between the sea surface and a depth of
400 m. Data sources, quality control and analysis procedures are
described in detail by Levitus et al. (1994), together with a detailed
distribution of temperature observations at the selected upper ocean
standard levels during the annual compositing period for each year
from 1960 to 1990.
Since we are interested in describing planetary-scale, basinwide
anomaly patterns on interannual and decadal time scales, we pre-
pared data sets on a 53 latitude-longitude grid by averaging the
temperature anomalies from the 13 latitude-longitude grid values.
The grid points are centered at 2.5 3N, 7.5 3N, 12.5 3N, etc., and at
122.5 3E, 127.5 3E, 132.5 3E, etc., respectively. Within the limitations
of the observed data (non-synoptic, scattered in space), the coarse
resolution chosen can include more data observations in the 53]53
grid box than in the original 13]13 grid box, thus giving a more
reliable estimate of observed temperature anomalies. Our previous
analyses (Zhang and Levitus 1996, 1997) indicate that this resolution
appears to be appropriate to describe interannual and decadal
patterns in the tropics and in the North Paci"c, respectively.
Heat content anomalies are calculated from the vertically aver-
aged yearly temperature anomalies in the upper 400 m of the water
column during 1961}90. In the EOF and regression analyses, the
value at individual gridpoints of the spatial EOF and regression
patterns is the eigenvalue of a covariance matrix and coe$cient of
linear regression between a selected reference time and the heat
content anomalies, respectively.

6 Oceanic links between the extratropics and tropics
6.1 Two di!erent interannual and decadal modes
Based on SST data, Deser and Blackmon (1995) and
Zhang et al. (1997) have shown that the decadal varia-
bility, which is dominant in the North Paci"c, can be
distinctly di!erent from interannual variability asso-
ciated with El Nin8 o in the tropics. The subsurface
evidence presented in this study further indicates that
the space-time evolution of decadal subsurface variabil-
ity is very di!erent from that of interannual variability,
suggesting that these can be two independent modes,
with their own structure and evolution characteristics,
and dynamics. As illustrated previously by other stud-
ies (e.g., Cane and Zebiak 1985), the tropical Paci"c
ocean-atmosphere system can generate its own mode of
interannual variability which has its principal signature
in the tropics (Fig. 2), while decadal variability appears
to involve the North Paci"c subtropical gyre. As sug-
gested by Latif and Barnett (1994, 1996), and Jin (1997),
the North Paci"c climate system can generate its own
mode of decadal variability that is independent of
tropical processes. Our basinwide data analyses, with
the interannual signal "ltered out, indicate that subsur-
face variability has a signi"cant decadal signature in
the tropics, suggesting that decadal tropical variability
is by no means entirely described by the El Nin8 o signal.
Thus, decadal variability in the Paci"c basin may not
be simply explained as an aggregate of the interannual
e!ect of tropical variability; instead it may originate
from the subtropical and midlatitude ocean where
there are potential processes with decadal time scales.
Nevertheless, there are clear connections between the
tropics and extratropics at subsurface depths.

The e!ect of tropical interannual variability on midlati-

6.3 The extratropical e!ects on the tropics
The oceanic links from the midlatitudes to the tropics
are the meridional subtropical cells (STCs), which have
been recently investigated by several theoretical and
modeling studies (e.g., McCreary and Lu 1994; Liu
et al. 1994; Liu and Philander 1995; Rothstein et al.
1998). The STCs form the basic pathways upon which
the extratropics and tropics exchange water, heat, and
other properties; the #ow is equatorward at subsurface
depth but poleward at the sea surface, connected by
subduction in the midlatitudes and upwelling on the
equator, respectively. Thus, extratropical temperature
anomalies can penetrate through the subtropics into
the tropics along #ow paths in the southern part of the
North Paci"c subtropical gyre. In due course, the sub-
duction of unusually warm or cold waters can in#uence
the thermocline structure in the subtropics and tropics
and eventually sea surface temperature on the equator,
which, in turn, a!ects the atmosphere (Gu and Philan-
der 1997). The extratropical anomalies may thus
change the mean thermal state (e.g., the depth of the
thermocline and zonal thermal gradient) and make
a direct contribution to low frequency variability in the
tropics. Since the tropical climate system is very sensi-
tive to these parameters (e.g., Cane and Zebiak 1985;
Zebiak 1987; Jin and Neelin 1993), the changes in the
zonal thermal structure and the depth of the thermo-
cline due to the external penetration of extratropical
anomalies into the tropics may modify the intensity of
ocean-atmosphere interactions in the natural tropical
system, potentially playing a role in the decadal-scale
behavior of El Nin8 o in the tropics.

7 Summary and discussion
Analyzing observed ocean heat content from 1961 to
1990 in the Paci"c (Levitus et al. 1994), we have investi-
gated the spatial patterns of the subsurface thermal
structure both on interannual and decadal time scales.
Their basinwide space-time evolution is further re-
vealed by using multivariate EOF and reconstructed
methods. Along with numerous well-documented stud-
ies of SST variability (e.g., Zhang et al. 1997), this
subsurface variability analysis may improve our under-
standing of interannual and decadal variability in the
Paci"c Ocean. It will be of value to verify the corre-
sponding results from theoretical analyses (e.g., Huang
and Pedlosky 1999) and numerical simulations of
ocean models as well as of coupled atmosphere-ocean
models (e.g., Latif and Barnett 1994, 1996; Yukimoto
et al. 1996; Knutson and Manabe 1998). However, the
data length of 30 y is obviously not long enough to
establish the statistical signi"cance of decadal patterns
in the Paci"c basin. Thus the inferred results are pre-
liminary and need to be further con"rmed by longer
data sets or more elaborate analysis techniques.
Subsurface anomalies show the clear coexistence of
decadal and interannual signals that apparently in-
volve the North Paci"c subtropical gyre and the
tropics, respectively. The di!erences in temporal evolu-
tion associated with interannual and decadal variations
suggest that they are distinctly di!erent from one an-
other. Interannual variability is associated with El
Nin8 o in the tropics, characterized by a phase propaga-
tion of subsurface anomalies in succession, eastward
along the equator, but westward o! the equator at
10}15 3N. Decadal signals are dominant in the midlati-
tudes and may not be necessarily connected with in-
terannual variations originating in the tropics; they can
be attributed to the North Paci"c subtropical ocean
gyre and air-sea interactions in the midlatitudes
